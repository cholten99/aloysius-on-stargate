Children of the Gods

 

Writers: Jonathan Glassner and Brad Wright

Director: Mario Azzopardi

 

Note: This review is written from the American DVD version of the pilot, which includes several scenes missing from the version shown on British TV.

 

And so the saga begins – in a fairly gentle manner with some soldiers bunking off to play poker in a forbidden area of a secret base.  My overactive brain starts questioning right away, though – what exactly is the function of this facility at this point?  It's been over a year since the supposedly only destination of the Stargate was supposedly blown up.  The soldiers say they’ve been shipping personnel out of the mountain for months, but why is there anyone there at all, I wonder?  And what do these soldiers actually do when they’re not playing poker?

 

Oh well, no time to ponder as Apophis is on his way.  It’s interesting that we get to see Teal’c before any of the others, although we’re probably not meant to realise at this stage how important a character he will turn out to be.  For particularly eagle-eyed viewers – and all of us watching for at least the second time – there are plenty of clues that he’s not exactly a happy Jaffa.  For example, when Apophis tells him to “Kree!” the look he throws at his god is positively murderous!

 

While the soldiers are getting summarily slaughtered, another question strikes – how does Apophis dial the gate to get back home?  There’s no DHD on Earth.  This will forever remain a mystery, however, since the scene cuts to marines rushing to the gate room and the outgoing wormhole is magically established when the gate is next on screen.  Apophis must have brought a Jaffa version of Carter with him, who dashed up to the control room and figured out how to set the dialling computer while we weren’t watching…

 

After the excitement of the opening scene, the first introduction of Jack is a little odd.  For most of the scene, we either get to see the back of his head or only half his face as he peers through his telescope.  Then, when Samuels mentions the Stargate and Jack turns to face the camera – wow!  I’m not usually a Jack kinda girl, but that close up leaves you in no doubt that you’ve just met the hero of this show!

 

Once on base, Jack shows us a side of his character that we didn’t see in the movie, but that will become increasingly familiar in the TV show.  He’s barely said two words to General Hammond and already he’s cracking jokes.  I love the way he looks round at Samuels when his joke fails to get a reaction from the general – tough crowd, Jack, you’re not going to get laughter from either of these guys!

 

Jack then goes on rather impressively to summarise the whole of his relationship with Daniel in the movie:

 

“Daniel was a scientist.”

“He sneezed a lot.”

“Basically, he was a geek, sir.”

“He saved my life and found a way home for my men and me.  Little thing like that kinda makes a person grow on you.”

 

And so we get nearly two hours of relationship development in four sentences.  A similar blink-and-you-miss-it revealing of back story occurs during the following scene between Jack and Kawalsky.  In a few short moments, we get the low down on Jack’s relationship with the Abydonian boy, Skaara, as well as a reminder of Jack’s personal tragedy – the death of his son.  It’s almost as if the writers are saying, “Look!  This is a sequel to the movie – honest it is!”

 

Then we get the first instance of something that quickly becomes the very backbone of Stargate SG-1 – Gary Jones as the technician at the gate controls, reciting the dialling sequence.  I’ve always felt really sorry for him – he’s in practically every episode and he very rarely gets to say anything else.  His character doesn’t even get a name!  But there he is, right from the start.

 

Main character number three, and the pilot scene I really have problems with.  I’ve always found the introduction of Sam rather stilted and a little over the top.  It just seems as if they’re trying too hard to present the “woman in a man’s profession” issue.  But then, I’ve never been a great advocate of feminism – I’ve had groups of male friends who’ve referred to me variously as “Beav’s Bird”, “Dave’s Sister” or simply “Woman!” and it’s never bothered me one bit.  So I’m probably not the best person to comment on this point!  I do think that the whole female soldiers thing is dealt with much better in the scenes between Sam and Janet in “Hathor”, though.

 

My other problem with this scene is that the way Jack says “I like women” sends a shiver down my spine.  He manages to make it sound like he’s a serial killer who skins them alive and then eats them!  I’m not sure what his tone is supposed to convey, but I’m not sure it’s that!

 

The characterisation of Sam improves no end when she first lays eyes on an active wormhole.  Her scientific enthusiasm and instant technobabble are very appealing and her wide-eyed wonder softens the previously hard edge to her personality.

 

Half an hour in and finally all the characters come into play.  Daniel steps into frame and the love affair begins.  One of my favourite things about the pilot is that we get to see Daniel happy for the first and only time in the whole five seasons.  The look he gives Sha’re as she comes to join him is so full of love and contentment, it always breaks my heart because I know what lies in store.

 

First possibility for the Jack/Daniel friendship to manifest itself, and what does Jack do?  He brushes straight past Daniel in order to go hug Skaara.  However, I can forgive him that, since it’s in keeping with the respective relationships from the film.  And when he finally does turn round to greet Daniel, there is real warmth in Jack’s tone.  The teasing from Kawalsky and Ferretti is nice, too, since it hearkens back to the way they tormented Daniel at the start of the film while at the same time demonstrating the real affection for him that had developed by the end.

 

I love Daniel’s expression when Sha’re finally comes up for air after kissing him in front of everybody.  He looks even more surprised about it than Jack does!

 

However, the best thing about the Abydos section is the interaction between Sam and Daniel – the Science Twins!  They are both so passionate about knowledge and they obviously find a real connection with each other.  They listen intently to one another as they gradually work out the cartouche, each adding another step to the theory in turn and even finishing one another’s sentences – it’s really cute.

 

In contrast, the cartouche room scene firmly establishes the professional relationship between Jack and Daniel.  Daniel enthusiastically begins to discuss his theories, expecting Jack to be able to pre-empt his explanation.  Jack gets impatient with him and demands a concise summary, clearly not interested in listening to Daniel expound.

 

However, Jack’s dismissal of Daniel when he’s trying to explain something doesn’t mean he is dismissing him as a person.  His expression of heartfelt sympathy as Daniel goes to say goodbye to the Abydonians is a clear indication of Jack’s compassion.  He’s not just a sarcastic, wise-cracking goofball – he really does care.

 

Daniel’s farewell speech is truly wonderful.  In less than five minutes, he’s lost his wife and is being forced to leave his home.  Michael Shanks does a fantastic job of expressing what Daniel is feeling here, and the way the Abydonians all reach out to him is a lovely touch in a very emotional scene.

 

Back to Earth, and the next question.  Did they really manage to make and fit such a complex mechanism as the iris in less than 24 hours?  Not likely!

 

Switching to Chulak, the characterisation of Teal’c becomes more obvious as he removes Sha’re from the holding cell.  He is forced by his position as First Prime to do this, but he clearly isn’t happy about it, and he stops Skaara from sacrificing himself needlessly, where most Jaffa would probably just have shot the boy.

 

Next comes a wonderful scene that was cut out of the version shown in Britain, and I’m extremely glad I bought the DVD because it’s wonderful.  Jack taking Daniel home is also very important for character establishment, background information and the development of the bond between the two men.

 

Brief aside: as Jack comes out of the kitchen, he is tossing one of the beer bottles round and round in his hand.  This is the bottle he gives to Daniel – does he want him to spray it all over the carpet when he opens it??

 

The final shot of this scene is very well constructed.  It shows Jack half reclining on his couch, completely relaxed and at ease in his home.  Daniel, on the other hand, is sitting forwards on the edge of a chair, elbows on his knees, looking very uncomfortable with being on Earth and in somebody else’s house.  I also love the fact that the clothes he has been given are far too big for him; it makes him look even more lost and vulnerable.

 

Moving to the other extreme of characterisation, I do think Apophis makes a great baddie.  The gold armour, the voice, the cold expression, the ruthless search for a host for his queen – it all combines to make him very sinister.  The way he looks up and down the Air Force Sergeant’s body is quite chilling.  We get our first sighting of a Goa’uld symbiote in this scene, too, and it’s suitably unpleasant.  The fact that Apophis refers to the creature as “my love” makes the whole thing even more disturbing, although the female Jaffa looks almost orgasmic as the symbiote pokes its head out of her pouch.  I don’t know what somebody’s doing to her off camera but she’s definitely enjoying it!

 

Then comes briefing room exposition – hurrah!  General Hammond makes his announcement about the formation of SG teams and we all go – aha!  Now we see how they’re going to get a TV series out of this scenario – of course!

 

Further important Jack characterisation occurs when Samuels gets the message that Ferretti is conscious.  Jack is out of the door before anyone else has even digested the information, and well before Hammond says, “Dismissed.”  The people under Jack’s command come before everything else, and he certainly isn’t a poster boy for military protocol!

 

Then it’s off to Chulak for our heroes and we get another thing that’s carried over from the film – going through a Stargate wormhole makes you very cold.  Jack actually has ice all over his face when he arrives on Chulak, but strangely this is no longer a problem in later episodes.

 

The implantation of Sha’re is quite shocking and actually fairly horrific so I can understand why it was cut from the TV version.  However, it does show how the Goa’uld/host relationship is formed, which is pretty important to the main plot of the series, so it’s better to have it there than not.

 

Over the top characterisation rears its ugly head once more on Chulak in the case of both Sam and Daniel.  Kawalsky and Jack are unnecessarily dismissive of Sam setting claymores on the ridge, and Sam is inevitably indignant at the discovery that Sha’re was a gift to Daniel.  Daniel himself doesn’t get very good presentation on Chulak, either, beginning the visit by sneezing and asking for tissues, and ending it by having to fumble through his notebook for the gate address to Earth while the team are under heavy fire.  It’s totally ridiculous to suggest that he wouldn’t have memorised the symbols for Earth before they left.  There are only seven of them, for heaven’s sake!  And it’s not as if the man has a bad memory – he’s fluent in 23 languages, after all!  But I’m getting ahead of myself…

 

At this point, we get a big continuity error.  When Hammond assigns Kawalsky as the leader of SG-2, he calls him Major but, on Chulak, Warren refers to him as Captain.  It seems more likely that he would be a major, as commander of the second team, but then the leader of SG-6 in “The First Commandment” is a captain, so who knows?

 

The connection between Teal’c and Jack is established very soon after their meeting, when the Goa’uld come to choose hosts for their children.  As the Jaffa force all the prisoners to kneel, Teal’c nods at Jack not to resist yet, demonstrating beyond all doubt that his allegiance to Apophis is less than absolute.

 

Jack’s plea to Teal’c for help and Teal’c’s decision to give it is a powerful moment and a great conclusion to all the hints of Teal’c’s character that have gone before.  Then, the flight to the gate establishes Jack and Teal’c as an effective fighting team, as they attempt to defend the refugees from the death gliders.

 

However, the fight at the gate goes on a little too long for my liking.  It all gets rather tedious after the first hundred or so staff weapon blasts.  It also highlights another continuity error.  In the very first scene, as well as the attack on Abydos, the armour of the Jaffa seems remarkably resistant to bullets.  Here, though, the rifles of the humans penetrate it with no problem.

 

Jack’s defence and support of Teal’c in the face of Hammond’s hostility is great and leads brilliantly to the establishing shot of the four members of SG-1 lined up on the ramp in front of the Stargate.  It’s crystal clear who this TV show is going to be about once we’ve seen that!

 

Finally, Kawalsky’s eye glow is possibly the scariest eye glow of all, and a chilling way to end the pilot.  There’s no doubt that this show is far from over!

 

So, in conclusion, this is pretty good as pilots go.  There’s lots of excitement, generally a good pace, promising character introductions, and it sets up the series very well.  However, it’s a bit dark and bloody for my tastes – I much prefer the more lighthearted tone the show settles down into after the first few episodes.

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

We get the first “Ya think?” moment, when the SGC doctor reveals the dead Jaffa and says, “They’re not human”.

 

We get not one, but two utterances of “For crying out loud!”  First of all during the scene in Jack’s house when Daniel professes to getting drunk: “Daniel, for crying out loud, you’ve had one beer!  You’re a cheaper date than my wife was.”  The second is muttered to Sam when the priests approach on Chulak and Daniel immediately leaps out to greet them.

 

Best Bits

 

Beginnings of the famous Jack/Daniel banter:

Daniel: They treated me like I was their saviour.  It was… embarrassing!

Jack: It’s amazing you turned out so normal.

 

Jack despairs of Daniel’s tendency to leap first and think later as he bounds out to say hi to the priests of Chulak:

Jack: The man has not changed.

 

Powerful character bonding with a Jack twist:

Jack: Come on!

Teal’c: I have nowhere to go.

Jack: For this, you can stay at my place!  Come on!
