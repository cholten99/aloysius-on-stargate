Thor’s Chariot

 

Writer: Katharyn Powers

Director: William Gereghty

 

An interesting start, where we join the team half way through Sam explaining their plan for finding the origin of the Goa’uld attack on Earth.  It’s interesting to see them going on the offensive in this way, instead of focusing solely on finding defensive technology.  Jack’s not really listening, though – he’s clearly lost concentration and is looking round about for something more interesting to do!

 

Once the plight of Cimmerians has been made evident, everyone inevitably repairs to the briefing room to discuss things.  The meeting seems rather stilted to me – they each take turns to remind us of what happened in Thor’s Hammer, recapping through dialogue rather than a montage of clips before the ep starts.  It is nearly a year since the original Cimmeria ep, but they all know what happened, so it seems odd for them to tell it to each other.

 

It’s quite interesting to see them having their version of a Prime Directive debate, though – finally concluding that it is their responsibility to help as it was them that caused the problem.  A different sort of episode – they’ve messed up for a change and have to try to rectify things.

 

When they arrive on the planet, Sam continues the trend of unnecessary comments, and her own habit of stating the obvious, with her observation, “No hostiles in sight, sir.”  I know the viewers can’t see that, because the camera is facing towards SG-1, but Jack can surely determine this for himself!

 

It’s nice to see Gerowyn again, although the circumstances are obviously less than favourable.  I love the way Gerowyn talks, using phrases like “flying the box through the portal”.  It’s an effective representation of somebody who is intelligent enough to grasp everything that goes on, but does not have the technical vocabulary to describe it.  She uses primitive terminology, but is never portrayed as being inferior.

 

When they ask to see Kendra, why doesn’t Gerowyn just tell them she’s dead!  I know it leads to their discovery that Sam can use Goa’uld technology, but it seems rather odd for Gerowyn to lead them on a long trek to visit some graves when the survivors among her people are in danger.  Her tone when she says, “They’ve already killed over half of our people” suggests that she wants SG-1 to realise the extent of the damage they have inadvertently caused, though.  Perhaps bringing them to see the graves is a way to emphasise this.

 

I love the way Daniel’s initial alarm at seeing Sam trying on the ribbon device turns immediately to fascination when she actually activates it!  But surely it shouldn’t be too much of a shock – they’ve already seen Kendra use the healing device, so it’s logical that Sam should have the capacity as well.

 

It’s excellent characterisation of Sam that her fear of the ribbon device is not that she can activate it, but rather that she cannot control it.  It’s a device of great destructive power, and she doesn’t like not being able to understand her ability to use it.

 

Then they split the party – Jack and Teal’c go off to play soldier in the long grass while Sam and Daniel investigate the local folklore.  This division works very well – Jack and Teal’c make an effective military team, while Sam and Daniel compliment each other with their differing areas of academic expertise.

 

I like the way the attitudes of the Goa’uld and Asgard are distinguished:

Heru’ur: Kneel before your god!

Olaf: My god is Thor.  He taught us to stand as equals.

This is a very effective demonstration of how the Goa’uld pose as gods to enslave, whereas the Asgard pose as gods in order to teach.

 

Jack’s attitude towards Daniel is very dismissive when he and Teal’c get back to the caves.  He isn’t interested in hearing what Daniel has to say, but immediately agrees to the plan when Sam chips in with her approval of it.

 

Then we get an unnecessary idiotic Daniel moment when he, Sam and Gerowyn are transported underground.  He switches on his torch and shines it directly into his face – I really don’t see how this adds to the episode, except to make Daniel look silly, which he isn’t.  Harumph!  Also, from this point onwards, I feel the urge to go down to the Hall of Thor’s Might with a comb and sort out Daniel’s hair – fuzzy is definitely not a good look on him!  Harumph again!

 

Thor’s beard goes rather too far in the other direction, I feel.  Ringlets don’t really suggest incredible might to me…

 

The beam test gives rise to another one-liner that has spawned a thousand fanfiction scenes – Daniel’s fear of heights is a common aspect of fanfiction, and this is where it originates.  It is evidently canon, but not really explored within the show.

 

By the time they reach the rune puzzle, Gerowyn has obviously been spending too much time with Sam.  Her question of “Is Thor testing us?” is rather unnecessary and, if Jack had been there, I can guarantee it would have elicited a response of – ya think?

 

Despite his dismissal of Daniel earlier on, Jack twice says, “Come on, Daniel” in a way that suggests he is putting great faith in Daniel to come up with the goods.  It seems strange that he should only mention Daniel on these occasions, when it was Sam’s argument that persuaded him to let them go, and he doesn’t know what they are facing in the Hall.

 

Sam is rather impatient with Daniel when they are faced with the runes.  She puts a lot of pressure on him to hurry up and doesn’t initially offer any assistance except to hassle him.  However, it eventually turns out to be a good job that they are both there, since the puzzle requires specialist knowledge from both of them in order to be solved.

 

And then of course we get our first glimpse of the real Thor – I just love him!  He is so cool, and so funky and so cute!!  I’m so glad they brought him back as a recurring character, because he’s great!

 

When Sam and Daniel return empty-handed, Jack again puts all the responsibility on Daniel and really takes out his frustration on the poor guy.  Various people have pointed out to me that Jack is more dismissive of Daniel in Season Four, but I see evidence of it here too.

 

Much like the Season Five episode Red Sky, SG-1 mess up, try to set things right, fail miserably and have to be saved by the Asgard.  The ending of this episode is deus ex machina in its most literal form – the gods descend from the sky in a big machine and save the day with their incredible powers!

 

Characteristic Moments

 

When faced with Olaf’s initial hostility:

Jack: For crying out loud, don’t you understand that this woman is right?

It’s interesting that he uses Olaf’s own phraseology, referring to Gerowyn as “this woman” rather than using her name – though it could just be that he can’t remember it!

 

When relating that the gate is heavily guarded:

Jack: I’m afraid we’re on our own, kids.

 

Best Bits

 

During the briefing:

Jack: You all know I take great pride in my title as Mr Postitive – however…

 

One of my all time favourite Stargate moments, made even better by the way they all step forwards in turn to form a line of guilt:

Teal’c: The destruction of the hammer device to save my life may have caused this.  If so, I am responsible.

Jack: General, I gave the order.

Daniel: And I fired the staff at the machine.

Sam: And I was – there…

I just love the way they all turn and look at her when she says this!

 

Discussing Heru’ur:

Teal’c: He is the son of Ra, and of Hathor.

Jack: Nice pedigree.

 

Daniel’s painful honesty:

Thor: Cimmeria is a safe world.

Daniel: Yeah, we sorta messed that up.

This is a great line, and I have it as a wav. file on my computer so that Daniel says it to me whenever I press the wrong button!
