Serpent’s Song

 

 

Writer: Katharyn Powers

Director: Peter DeLuise

 

An unusual start to a fairly unusual episode – we open in space with a death glider being shot at by mother ships – intriguing!

 

Then we get the whole team in desert cammies – woohoo!  It doesn’t get better than that, unless Daniel swaps his silly hat for a bandanna, of course.  Daniel’s lack of military background is highlighted yet again, with him lounging on the steps of the Stargate in a rather relaxed fashion, while Jack and Sam sit upright with their guns close at hand.

 

I love the shot of Teal’c just standing there and watching as the death glider careens towards him amidst blasts of laser cannons – what a guy!

 

Jack coins a new phrase when he sees that the death glider’s pilot is Apophis.  I realise he must be somewhat surprised, but “Holy Buckets”???  What’s that about?

 

The intrigue of the opening is increased by the sight of a rather battered Apophis lying in the sand, begging our heroes for help – certainly not something I ever thought I’d see, but nicely satisfying!

 

Jack stepping backwards into the wormhole while firing his rifle, and the camera tracking back through to see him fall out of the other side is a really cool effect, and not one we’ve seen before.

 

I like the way Jack introduces Hammond to Apophis, and Hammond says, “We’ve met.”  I’d forgotten that Hammond is actually the first of the main characters to come across Apophis – he turns up in the gate room during Apophis’ attack at the start of Children of the Gods, so he’s more than familiar with everyone’s favourite snakehead.

 

Having their arch enemy at their mercy is an interesting premise for an episode – not something that happens overly much in TV shows.  It’s also a great opportunity to introduce another Goa’uld, in the form of Sokar.  The idea that the big baddie of the show is actually running away from someone else is a great way to present a new level of threat.

 

Daniel: He gambled we would show compassion even to our worst enemy.

Just described yourself to a tee there, honey!  But there’s actually a distinct lack of compassion directed at Snakeboy during the rest of the episode!  Teal’c, for example, is positively bouncing around with a huge smug grin on his face at this point – good to know that he’s susceptible to gloating!

 

There’s an interesting range of attitudes in this episode, though:

Sam focuses on the practical – what information can they get?

Daniel’s battling his personal demons with regard to Apophis, and is clearly uncomfortable with his hatred.

Teal’c is supremely satisfied to have his former ‘god’ at his mercy.

Janet reacts like the doctor she is, looking out for the wellbeing of her patient.

Jack just wants him dead, as soon as possible.

 

This leads to a great range of interaction with Apophis during the so-called interrogation:

Jack plays ultimate hardball with him – he doesn’t care in the least if Apophis dies and certainly isn’t prepared to bargain with him.  Janet seems quite shocked by Jack’s harshness.

Daniel’s announcement that he knows the location of the baby is so quiet and yet completely laced with steel – he really shows his more ruthless side in his conversation with Apophis.  There’s no way he’s bluffing when he threatens to kill Apophis, and not the tiniest bit of pity in his eyes when Apophis is in pain.  He may be the first to show compassion in the worst of circumstances, but this is one enemy who will get absolutely nowhere with him.

Sam has a practical attitude towards keeping Apophis alive as long as possible, and confines her questions to technology.

Teal’c actually looks pleased to see his old master in pain, and takes immense pleasure in gloating over his incapacitation, even going so far as to suggest witholding his pain relief – a hard Jaffa indeed.

 

Then we get the introduction of Sokar – one of the creepier bad guys around, as Daniel tells us:

Daniel: Basically, he was the original Satan.

Jack: Well, isn’t that special.

 

Lieutenant Simmons is on gate duty when the Tok’ra arrive, and I notice he indulges in a little glance of longing at Sam as she joins him at the controls – obviously still burning a torch for her!

 

Then we get another of Sam’s fans in the form of Martouf – nice to see him again so soon, and what a cute smile!  Lantash shows off his more typical Tok’ra side by being rather aggressive in the briefing room, but he does it out of concern for the Tau’ri, and there’s no hint of condescension in his words, so I think we can let him off!

 

Sokar’s first appearance is typically cliched – you will be destroyed, yadda, yadda – heard it all before, really!  Do bad guys not listen to themselves?

 

Sergeant Siler plays his usual role of trying to fix stuff when it’s all going horribly wrong – what a guy!  I don’t think the silver suit is a good fashion statement for him, though…

 

I like the way Jack acts as a middleman between Daniel and Hammond, telling Hammond about Daniel’s idea to ensure it’s given the proper attention.  I guess it’s still early enough that Hammond doesn’t automatically realise that Daniel’s ideas are always brilliant!  Nice to see such strong support from Jack, too.

 

Daniel is really beautiful when speaking words of comfort in Ancient Egyptian to Apophis’ host, and there’s a rather lovely shot of Jack watching him, too – a very nice scene.

 

One thing I don’t understand is why the heat pattern on the iris is so geometrical – wouldn’t it just be a big red splodge gradually spreading out from the middle, rather than a precise spiral?

 

And another thing – at the end, Martouf taps a few letters on the keyboard of the dialling computer – how on earth does this translate into a stargate address?  Well, I guess it doesn’t translate into anything on Earth, but hey – you know what I mean!

 

Not an especially jolly episode, not especially thrilling, and certainly not the end of Apophis!  If there’s one thing we’ve learned from Stargate so far, it’s that death isn’t necessarily final!  There’s some interesting exploration of the main characters, but it’s not one of the better episodes, in my opinion.

 

As evidenced by –

 

No Great Moments – even the worst eps usually have one of two classic bits – but not this one!  Strictly average.
