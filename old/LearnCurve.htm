Learning Curve

 

 

Writer: Heather E Ash

Director: Martin Wood

 

Not one of my favourite eps – far too many kids involved for my liking!  Although, making them work for a living from a very early age and then sucking out their brains seems like a good idea to me!  (Only joking…)

 

It’s great to see Daniel getting all excited about archaeological excavation and actually being allowed to play.  Jack seems a little jealous that all three of his kids have stuff to do – but of course he gets to share his expertise in being a child soon enough!

 

Sam’s expression at receiving the reactor is positively rapturous – she really does love machines, doesn’t she?  And it’s probably a good job, considering her track record with men…

 

Jack’s incredulity at Merrin being a reactor expert is funny, but it demonstrates yet again that all the things they’ve seen through the Stargate haven’t made them even one iota more open-minded.  It shows how different Daniel’s mindset is, as well, since he doesn’t question the use of the children, he just accepts it as part of the culture.

 

The sexiness of the bandanna, coupled with Daniel’s enthusiasm for what he’s doing, is unfortunately ruined somewhat by his safety specs in the early part of this ep.  We get a wonderful example of Daniel in excitable puppy mode when Kalan interrupts him and tells him to relate his findings to the young boy – he just switches focus to the new audience and carries on.  He’s probably so amazed that someone is prepared to listen to him that he doesn’t care who it is!

 

For a doctor, Janet’s not very good at hiding her concern.  The exchange of glances between her and Sam when she comes to collect Merrin is great – but her reassuring smile to Merrin leaves a lot to be desired.

 

I have to say that Jack’s looking particularly yummy in this ep – he looks good in his black sweater, and I’m happy to give him some attention when it’s due.  See?  I’m not so blinded by my love for Daniel that I can’t appreciate others!

 

Sam’s pride in her stick figure drawing is very sweet, but Merrin is less than impressed, and poor Sam seems rather embarrassed that an eleven-year-old thinks she’s rubbish at drawing.  Sam’s never been very good a accepting people who are smarter than her, and having trouble comprehending the scientific knowledge of a child proves quite a hurdle for her to overcome.

 

Back offworld, the geekiness of the safety specs is entirely compensated by the sight of Daniel wielding a hammer – yum!  This is Daniel totally in his element, which is always good to see.  (I never said I’d stop commenting on how wonderful Daniel is – just that I’d throw a few compliments Jack’s way, too…)

 

We then get to see Teal’c ‘feeling strongly’ about things again, which is also always good.  His demand to see Tomin after he’s gone through the Ovarium is very compelling.  Teal’c hates the idea of anyone being mistreated, and once his suspicions have been raised, there’s no stopping him.

 

The scene at the Orbanian gate between Daniel and Kalan is really interesting.  SG1’s gut reaction against the Ovarium is very strong – which is understandable, but you can see Kalan’s point of view as well.  It’s also understandable that he objects to them suggesting their entire way of life his wrong – tricky situation!

 

Technobabble ahoy – is the answer to every sci-fi scientific problem to reverse the polarity??  I do like Sam’s insistence on working it out for herself, though.

 

It seems Daniel’s been hanging around Jack too long – in Hammond’s office, he’s fiddling with one of Hammond’s desk ornaments – a definite sign of his discomfort with the situation, but also very much a Jack habit.

 

I love the idea that Jack visits the school often enough for the kids to know him and be excited about him being there – it’s really cute.  The teacher is very sweet – she seems to have an understanding of Jack’s love for children, and is genuinely pleased at his interest.  I just love the painting of Jack that one of the kids does in the art class, too – and Jack’s interaction with all the kids is great.

 

And yet again, Jack makes friend with a child and then loses them – it’s almost as bad as Sam’s prospective boyfriends dying!

 

It all kind of works out for the best in the end (though I’m not sure about the educational value in graffiti, but hey…) – but this could so easily have been a pre-cursor to The Other Side, where we see what happens when the differences between societies can’t be resolved.  I guess it’s the whole Prime Directive issue again – do we have the right to dictate to other people how they should live?  Not an easy debate to take on.

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Jack’s teaching of Merrin gets quite aggressive, considering he’s trying to teach the art of having fun:

Jack: Paint me a flower.  And don’t stop with just one – paint the whole garden.

Merrin: But –

Jack: Ah! [with the ubiquitous finger] Paint!

 

 

Best Bits

 

Merrin’s perceptions are right on the money:

Sam: School is a place where you go to learn.  A teacher gives you information and you take different classes to learn different subjects.

Merrin: So this is how you and Dr Fraiser became scientists?

Sam: Mm-hmm.

Merrin: [to Jack] You aren’t a scientist?

Jack: Oh, no.

Merrin: Then you are not as smart as Major Carter and Dr Fraiser.

[Jack’s eyebrow twitches and Sam looks at the floor.]

Jack: Well, it depends on what you mean by –

[Sam looks sideways at Jack, a huge grin plastered all over her face.]

Jack: Okay, no – I’m not.

 

Sam thinks sleep is a necessary evil:

Jack: You’ve been at this for 24 hours.  You need rest.

Merrin: I do not need to.  Urrone children require little sleep.

Sam: Okay, now I am jealous.

 

The excitement of success goes to Sam’s head:

Hammond: In the future, Major, before you activate any device that includes the word ‘reactor’, I would appreciate if you would notify me.
