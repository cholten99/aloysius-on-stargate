Rules of Engagement

 

 

Writer: Terry Curtis Fox

Director: William Gereghty

 

Yet another ‘we’re searching for a downed UAV’ opening – I think that makes a total of three now.  However, I guess it’s fairly realistic that it would fail (or get shot down) every now and then.

 

Sam’s got her ‘stating the obvious’ hat on again, announcing “Weapons fire!” after they hear guns in the distance.  Thanks, Sam – not sure we would have figured that out for ourselves…

 

I’ve always thought the premise of this ep is pretty cool – discovering an SG team on a random planet is an intriguing opening, and then finding out they’re really servants of Apophis learning infiltration techniques makes it all the more interesting.  Just goes to show that Apophis did have some good ideas sometimes and wasn’t completely restricted to cliched megalomaniac behaviour!

 

Daniel’s getting better at the soldier thing (he crawls down the hill quite happily with everyone else) but his instincts aren’t quite fully honed yet – he waits until they’re right on the edges of the battle before he fumbles to draw his gun.  Shows progression, though – good character development.

 

Watching apparent SG personnel take out SG-1 is pretty creepy, and makes for a great pre-credits teaser.

 

Daniel’s ever so cute when he’s confused, and he looks confused throughout most of the early part of this episode.

 

Jack’s usual irritating sarcasm comes fully into play – as much a sign of confusion as the line between Daniel’s eyebrows.  Jack really doesn’t like to have information withheld from him, but he does like to annoy the hell out of those who withhold it!

 

I love the way Kyle interprets their reaction to his saying ‘chappa’ai’ as him having failed their test.  I really like the whole presentation of his mindset throughout the episode – it’s a fascinating exploration of the effects of Apophis’ manipulation of him.

 

Teal’c really seems to enjoy getting the chance to play Jack’s superior for a change – his wholehearted leap into character is very amusing!  Daniel, on the other hand, isn’t quite as comfortable in his role – his acting skills leave a lot to be desired and he goes a little bit over the top in his attempt to be commanding.

 

Kyle: Shall I have him sent for?

Daniel: No, I don’t think that will be necessary.

Teal’c: Send for him.

Daniel: [rather forcefully] Yes!  Send for him!

 

I think Teal’c really like surprising Jack.  He takes every opportunity to do so – but the best one is when he tells the soldiers Jack is much loved by Apophis.  Jack’s reaction is really funny!

 

Daniel can be really sneaky when he wants to be.  His argument to persuade Kyle to go back with them is very clever – praying exactly on Kyle’s weaknesses and using his own stubborn adherence to his rules against him.

 

Oodles of disapproval fill the briefing room when Teal’c describes The Final Challenge.  His tone and facial expressions are really interesting – he knows Apophis is evil, but he still has great pride in being victorious at his own Final Challenge.  He’s conflicted between agreeing with the rest of SG-1 that Jaffa training is wrong, and defending the process by which he achieved his warrior status.  It’s an interesting dilemma.

 

Jack’s ‘infamous tuna torture’ sequences is really funny.  His usual immediate bond with younger people doesn’t quite work with Kyle, but it starts to take effect eventually, leaving Jack’s track record intact.

 

Kyle shows good leadership skills for one so young – he is extremely loyal to his men and always acts in what he perceives to be their best interests.

 

I am a little confused as to how the soldiers were supposed to infiltrate Earth – how was Apophis proposing to get them there undetected?

 

I like the way Jack puts his faith in Kyle and supports his request to General Hammond to join them on their return to the planet.  It’s a nice touch.

 

Then we get gratuitous Daniel-bashing:

Jack: Is everyone clear on that?  Daniel?

Daniel: What?

Jack: Good.

 

I mean, is this really necessary?  The implication here is (a) that Jack assumes Daniel won’t understand military strategy and (b) that he thinks it doesn’t really matter anyway – an extremely dangerous attitude to have when going into a hostile situation and also incredibly unfair to Daniel!  Grrrr!

 

Daniel actually acquits himself very well during the assault on the camp.  He takes cover behind the sandbags like a pro (which I guess he now is!) and, while his aim is not spectacular, he does manage to take out quite a few of the attackers with his entar.

 

It’s a fairly downbeat ending, but not a bad episode overall – interesting premise, some funny moments, and a thought-provoking theme.

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Jack’s not impressed by the trainees’ stubbornness:

Daniel: As a matter of fact, there’s probably nothing we can do for him as long as we stay here – there’s probably internal injuries.

Jack: So we’ll take him back with us.

Nelson: You cannot!

Jack: For crying out loud.

 

 

Best Bits

 

The first soldier they meet obviously touches a nerve with Daniel:

Daniel: We’re just trying to understand.  If we’re supposed to be dead, how are you supposed to kill us again?

Soldier: You wanna find out, Four-Eyes, huh?

Daniel: [incredulous and slightly annoyed] Four-Eyes?

Jack: Easy, big fella.

 

Teal’c gets into his role as Jaffa Master:

Jack: Master Teal’c – might I suggest we spare them this time?

Teal’c: Very well – underling.

 

Jack’s cleverer than even he knows:

Carter: Then Colonel O’Neill’s already thought of the solution.

[Everybody look incredulously at Jack, who just looks confused.]

Carter: I think you know where I’m going with this, sir.

Jack: Haven’t a clue.
