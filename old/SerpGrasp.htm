Within the Serpent’s Grasp

 

Story: James Crocker

Teleplay: Jonathan Glassner

Director: David Warry-Smith

 

Ah, the ritual shredding of the top secret documents!  It’s got to really piss off the Reprographics Department when the SGC is reopened, having to replace all that paperwork!

 

The body language of the other three when Jack comes to join them in the control room is very interesting.  Sam and Teal’c are sitting down, slumped in resignation.  Daniel, on the other hand, is standing up, his fist clenched and banging against his leg.  He tracks Jack’s progress across the room, his expression set – there is no way he is giving up this easily.

 

Teal’c is up for Daniel’s plan right away, but I think it’s understandable that it takes the other two a little longer to come round to the idea.  It’s not that they don’t want to support him, it’s just that they both have a hell of a lot more to lose than Daniel or Teal’c, and they’re trained not to disobey orders.  Teal’c is a soldier, too, but Bra’tac has specifically prepared him to question everything and do what he believes to be right, no matter what his superiors tell him differently.

 

Teal’c does his version of a huge grin when Jack agrees to go along – this is the happiest I think I’ve ever seen him, except perhaps for the morning after the night before in Crossroads in Season Four…

 

When they actually set off, we finally get to see Sam in a hat that doesn’t suit her.  The woolly black affair does nothing for her at all, but then it doesn’t look too good on Daniel or Teal’c either.  I notice Jack gets his usual baseball cap – obviously he just flat refused to go with the woolly look.

 

I would have thought the ‘unauthorised activation’ alarm would go off when the dialling sequence starts rather than when it finishes.  It takes the SGC computer a very long time to dial, and it seems a bit far-fetched that they would be able to manage this without getting stopped, but hey!

 

I love the little sideways glance Feretti throws at the back of Hammond’s head after Teal’c disappears through the gate.  You can just see him wishing he’d been invited to go along with them so he didn’t have to face the backlash on Earth.

 

First introduction of zat guns!  And Teal’c gets to do the explaining for once, with his little lecture on “Firearms of the Goa’uld”.

 

Just before the ship’s engines start up and just before SG-1 get knocked over by the jump to hyperspeed, Jack turns his torch around and shines it directly into his own face – very bizarre!  I know he likes to fiddle with stuff, but this is a bit weird even for him!

 

This episode really highlights how little Daniel’s glasses make sense.  Michael Shanks doesn’t wear glasses, and Daniel’s use of them is never very consistent.  Most of the time he wears them, but he doesn’t seem to be hampered in any way on the occasions when he doesn’t.  For example, he doesn’t wear them at all in this episode, and yet he still spots the night vision goggles left on the floor on the opposite side of the gate room when the Jaffa come in to set up the communication device.  I thought for a bit that maybe he’s wearing contacts, but he does have his glasses with him (when he and Sam plant C4 on the gliders, he removes them from the neck of his T-shirt and puts them in one of his pockets for no apparent reason) and he puts them near the start of the next episode.

 

After Teal’c demonstrates that a third shot from a zat disintegrates the victim, Jack asks him why he didn’t mention this before, as if this was a really important omission.  What does it matter?  The second shot kills, so why would you care whether or not you were subsequently disintegrated?

 

I have some confusion issues about the whole gate on a ship thing, but I’m not sure if I’m just being stupid…  The fact that the SGC can’t establish a wormhole to Daniel’s co-ordinates means that the planet the ship was orbiting doesn’t have a Stargate, right?  That’s fair enough, I suppose.  But what I don’t get is how you can use a gate on a ship to get somewhere else.  Each planet uses its point of origin as the seventh symbol when dialling out, and this is generally a unique symbol on the DHD, right?  So a generic DHD on a ship wouldn’t have those symbols on it – or would it have a “point of origin” button that would serve like a skeleton key?  Would that even be possible?  I have no idea and, as I say, I’m probably just being stupid, so feel free just to ignore this paragraph, since I’m not sure it makes sense anyway!

 

The team’s attitude to their surroundings leaves rather a lot to be desired.  They’re on an enemy ship, full of enemy soldiers, they’ve just been taken by surprise by a guard, and they’re still meandering about a room with an occupied sarcophagus in it, chatting away at full volume and paying not attention whatsoever to the door!  Anybody heard of ‘stealth’ or ‘covert’?

 

Apophis gets to reveal his double whammy of screwing with Jack and Daniel.  Not content with using Daniel’s wife as a host for his queen, he puts his offspring in Jack’s surrogate son, who is also Daniel’s brother-in-law.  Poor Skaara – he does look good as a Goa’uld prince, though.

 

It’s all very well Jack and Teal’c sealing themselves in the cockpit so they can have a nice chat with Klorel/Skaara, but they have kinda sealed themselves in the cockpit…  I can’t imagine how they thought this scene was going to end without them being captured.  Maybe they didn’t – I just don’t quite see what Jack thinks he’s going to achieve here.  He surely can’t think he’ll be able to free Skaara from Klorel’s control, can he?

 

Alexis Cruz does a great job of switching back and forth from Klorel to Skaara.  His wig doesn’t quite stand up to the rough and tumble on the floor with Teal’c though – gets a bit skew wiff towards the end!

 

TV shows like this always make the military look bad.  One of the main reasons the heroes always defeat the bad guys is because they think for themselves and question their orders, whereas evil minions just do what they’re told and thus get into lots of trouble.  For example, it seems fairly obvious to me that Klorel is being influenced by his host when he tells the Jaffa not to kill Teal’c – perhaps because it’s totally against Apophis’ orders and in line with what the other prisoner has just told him to do!  But the minions don’t question, they just do what they’re told, thus giving our thinking heroes a chance to escape.

 

Daniel gets to do his Action!Jackson bit, looking very cool with two pistols when he and Sam come to rescue the other two.

 

I remember being really upset the first time I saw this episode and Jack was forced to shoot Skaara.  This ep is still early enough in the show that you don’t automatically expect people to come back to life again!  Of course, by the time you are expecting them to come back, they start really dying – that’s what makes the deaths of Sha’re and Martouf so powerful, the fact that you expect them to be revived but they stay dead.

 

Anyway, the end-of-season cliffhanger is pretty cool – Skaara is dead, SG-1 are trapped on an enemy ship that is about to blow up Earth – not bad for suspense over the summer…

 

Best Bits

 

Teal’c’s tendency to take his time over his sentences leads to an owie for Jack:

Teal’c: O’Neill!  Prepare yourself for –

            (whoosh! Thud of Jack’s head hitting the wall)

- extreme deceleration.

Jack: Yeah, thanks, Teal’c.
