Singularity

 

Writer: Robert C Cooper

Director: Mario Azzopardi

 

This episode has a great opening with the role reversal of Jack knowing about something for a change while Daniel is the one looking confused and asking all the questions.  Daniel’s total shock when Jack mentions the Acretian Disc (I really ought to look that up so I know how to spell it, but I can’t be bothered – sorry!) is really funny.  Jack’s reaction to the surprise is interesting – he looks embarrassed and tries to dismiss what he’s said, as if he didn’t mean to let slip that he actually has knowledge in his seldom-revealed brain.  I guess he wouldn’t want to look like he’s showing off and using terms the others don’t know, which is something he hates when Sam and Daniel do it.

 

Trust humans to plant a sign by the Stargate proclaiming the planet to be an SGC project site.  For whose benefit has this sign been erected, exactly?  It faces the gate so it’s clearly meant to be seen by people coming through, but surely all the SGC personnel know the project site is there!

 

It’s interesting how Jack says, “Daniel!  You and Teal’c check the village,” when they split up.  It almost sounds like he’s putting Daniel in command over Teal’c.  I guess there should always be a clear chain of command but I would have thought Jack would always put Sam in command of the other team when they split up.  She is the 2IC of the unit, after all.

 

This episode marks Janet’s first trip offworld, I believe.  It must be hard for her – going offworld must be an exciting prospect, but she only gets to do it when there’s a medical catastrophe on another planet!

 

And then we have the introduction of the next in a long line of child characters, this one obviously the most important, since she re-occurs in several episodes.  I really like Cassandra, especially since her presence allows us to see the sweetest sides of all the other characters.  Her first scene is a rare chance for Teal’c to show how sweet he can be – the way he entices Cassie out of the bushes is really wonderful and reminds us that there is a kind and compassionate Jaffa under that serious exterior.

 

Cassandra’s presence makes this my favourite Sam episode.  The progression of her attachment to Cassie is beautifully done, from slight bewilderment and uncertainty in the face of Cassie’s blind devotion through to selfless refusal to leave Cassie alone at the end.  I like the way Cassie’s effect on the other characters isn’t just confined to the women though – Jack, Daniel and Teal’c are just as smitten as Sam and Janet.

 

This episode is also very good for little touches that run all the way through.  The way Sam and Daniel jump Cassie through the gate near the beginning is echoed by them swinging her between them in the park at the end.  There’s also the repetition of Cassie’s obsession with the corpse tag, as well as the “very brave” refrain.

 

I think Amanda Tapping does a great job in the early scenes with Cassie.  It must be hard to play scenes where you have to do all the talking because the other character either can’t or won’t speak, and she does it very well.  The characterisation of Sam is very effective too – I like her expression of slight frustration when Cassie won’t let her leave.  It shows that Sam isn’t accustomed to situations like this (which she wouldn’t be) and helps demonstrate the development of her relationship with Cassie.

 

When she’s theorising about the cause of the infection on Hanka, Janet points out one of the biggest general plot holes in the show.  The likelihood that bacteria from Earth will cause unexpected damage on an alien planet, or vice versa, is huge, but they don’t normally have a decontamination process when people come back through the gate and they never take precautions not to affect other planets.  The Season Two ep One False Step explores this idea a bit further but, like here, it turns out that foreign bacteria aren’t actually to blame.

 

Jack’s explanation of the nature of black holes is great.  Daniel is a natural teacher, taking every opportunity to explain things to people in easily understandable terms.  Sam loves explaining things, too, but she’s not always very good at doing so in terms others can grasp.  She tends to get carried away with her enthusiasm and fall back into technobabble.  Jack, on the other hand, is a reluctant teacher.  He doesn’t explain the black hole until Teal’c asks, and then he does so very badly, stumbling over his sentences and utterly failing to convey any useful information.

 

Sam’s reaction to Cassandra’s collapse in the infirmary shows further progression in their relationship.  She must be trained in field medicine, but she panics and just stands there asking Janet what she should do.

 

Then we get the first of my two favourite ever Sam/Daniel moments.  I love the way Daniel is sitting outside Cassie’s room when Sam comes out (pause for a quick YUM!).  Then there’s wonderful progression in Sam’s attitude from defensively rejecting Daniel’s help to expressing appreciation for his offer of support.  His expression throughout and the way she runs her hand down his arm before walking away show how close they are, and it’s very sweet.  Moments like this between these two are particularly nice, since it gives Sam an opportunity to lose the constant military edge she has to maintain with the other men.

 

The other great Sam/Daniel moment comes after the briefing in which they present their Goa’uld treachery theory to General Hammond.  In the briefing, Sam is all professionalism and Daniel presents the emotional reaction to the situation.  However, as soon as they are alone, Sam’s detachment slips and Daniel is the one to remain clam in order to offer her support.  His friendship role continues into the next scene where he is seen leaning in the doorway while Sam talks to Hammond, then he moves to stand behind her when Hammond decides to send Cassie back through the gate.  There’s a final touch in this vein when Hammond later goes off to arrange the transfer of Cassie to the nuclear facility.  The camera zooms in to capture Sam’s reaction but you can see Daniel turn to look at her just before he disappears from the shot.

 

In contrast to the emotional support role, Daniel also has a weird “selfish” role to play in this episode.  Early on when Janet comes to tell them her findings on the infection, he interrupts her, saying, “Excuse me, I hate to sound self-centred here…”, prompting her to tell them they aren’t infected.  Then, later, when Sam experiments on the potassium and naquadah, releasing a whole load of radiation, Daniel’s immediate reaction is, “Are we safe?”  Now I’m not saying there’s anything  wrong with this attitude – far from it, it’s perfectly natural and a refreshing change from TV characters’ usual total disregard for their own safety – but both these moments always strike me as sounding rather strange coming from Daniel.

 

Question:  Why on earth does Teal’c stop to explain Nirti’s deceptive nature when he and Jack are on their way tot he Stargate under heavy fire?  I realise we need to be told about this before the others try to take Cassie back through the gate, but it does seem a rather inappropriate moment for Teal’c to suddenly get talkative!

 

Jack gets to show his supportive side, too, when they reach the nuclear facility, but he has to do in a commanding officer type fashion, offering to take Cassie down to the bottom and then carefully detailing how long Sam has to get back up.  He uses his authority over her to try to protect her by ordering her to come back to the surface but, at the moment of greatest stress, he abandons military protocol and calls her Sam.

 

There’s a great team solidarity moment when Jack tells Daniel and Teal’c to leave and they just look at him.  Poor Jack!  Nobody does what he tells them in this episode!

 

Then we have a majorly soppy ending, with Sam and Daniel presenting the perfect nuclear family, swinging Cassie between them, Jack making up rules about kids having to have dogs, and Teal’c suggesting that Sam ought to volunteer to be Cassie’s mother.  It’s all very sweet and – finally! – Daniel gets some decent clothes for a change!

 

All in all, I like this episode very much.

 

Best Bits

 

Opening banter:

Sam: During the totality phase of the eclipse, we should be able to see matter spiralling towards it.

Jack: Actually it’s called the Acretian Disc.

Daniel: Well, I guess it’s easy to understand why the local population would be afraid of something like that – what did you just say?

Jack: [suddenly all modest] It’s just an astronomical term.

Sam: You didn’t think the colonel had a telescope on his roof just to look at the neighbours did you?

Jack: Not initially…

Teal’c: [raises one eyebrow]

 

After Cassie’s operation:

Daniel: How is she?

Sam: She’s fine.  Sleeping.

Daniel: Um… if you want, I can sit with her tomorrow for a few hours.

Sam: No, we’re okay.

Daniel: Okay.

Sam: I just… I want to do this.

Daniel: Okay, but I guess what I’m saying is, you don’t have to do this alone.

Sam: Thanks [and she runs her hand down his arm – cute!]

 

Watching Cassie after they’ve figured out that it’s a Goa’uld plot:

Sam: How could they do this?

Daniel: To a Goa’uld, she’s not as we see her.  She’s a tool.  Her death is a very cheap way to get rid of us.

Sam: I know I’m supposed to be detached…

Daniel: Who said that?

Sam: Sometimes I forget you’re not military.

I always think the cut here seems a little abrupt, which makes me wonder if there was originally more to this scene.  I know I want Daniel to give Sam a big hug here…
