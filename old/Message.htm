Message In A Bottle

 

Story: Michael Greenburg + Jarrad Paul

Teleplay: Brad Wright

Director: David Warry-Smith

 

No matter how many time I watch this episode, the opening always takes me by surprise.  It’s unique in being a planet that can’t support human life but that they visit anyway.  And despite the fact that they play the whole scene in space suits (which I always think must be quite hard), all four contribute their typical responses to an alien situation:

 

·        Sam spouts technobabble and Jack pretends to understand her.

·        Daniel comes up with a peaceful and academic idea as to what the orb might be

·        Teal’c suggests the possibility of it being a trap, ever paranoid due to experiences with the Goa’uld

·        Jack’s attention seems to wander while Daniel is giving his thoughts, and then he asks the opinion of the other two, pretty much ignoring Daniel altogether

 

When they wander off back to the gate, the MALP trails behind them.  One of them is presumably controlling it, since there is no evidence that they are in contact with the SGC, in which case shouldn’t they send it out first so that they can see where they’re directing it?

 

On arrival back on Earth, it seems that Hammond is in a particularly good mood, since he actually smiles at Jack’s “one small step” joke.  He doesn’t usually even acknowledge Jack’s wisecracks, and this one is a lot less funny than most.

 

Then we get the introduction of Lieutenant Simmons, and isn’t he just the most adorable minor character going???  He is such a sweetheart, and also living proof that it’s only guys Sam fancies back that get killed off, though it obviously looks bad for him for a while later on…

 

Sam and Daniel unite in their desire to study the orb, but the difference in their backgrounds comes to the fore when Hammond says they can have 24 hours.  Daniel, of course, tries to argue further that this is not enough time, whereas Sam, in the military vein, graciously accepts what is offered, knowing that arguing won’t do them any good.

 

The whole scene of the next morning is great.  It starts with Daniel making a breakthrough on the translation, only to discover that he can’t use any of the information.  I love the way he throws his pencils down in frustration, and it’s nice the way Sam lays a hand on his shoulder in understanding.  Then Jack’s entrance is so deliberately obnoxious with his cheerful greeting and, again, the military vs civilian divide is emphasised.  When Jack orders them to get some sleep, Sam automatically acknowledges the order and immediately move to obey, while Daniel tries to argue and only gives in with bad grace.

 

Question: where on earth does the orb keep those huge long spiky poles when it’s not using them to skewer people?  Well, obviously, it doesn’t usually keep them on Earth, but hey…

 

And so begins the worst Jack-whumping in all five seasons.  My mum hates it so much, she can’t bear to watch the episode any more, but it’s never really bothered me.  I’m obviously just desensitised to such things by now.

 

More classic responses to the new situation: an emotional one from Daniel, who exclaims “Oh my god”; a medical one from Janet who is immediately fascinated that there is no blood; a loyal one from Teal’c with his determination to try whatever may be necessary to free his friend; and a scientific one from Sam, who focuses at once on the practicalities and tries to analyse what’s going on.

 

In contrast to his usual civilian naivety, it’s Daniel who voices cynicism in the face of the NID request to see the orb:

 

Hammond: The NID people want to see it.

Sam: Even knowing what it’s done to the Colonel.

Daniel: Especially knowing what it’s done to the Colonel.

 

There’s a lot of guilt flying about in this episode.  Daniel claims some here by saying that he wanted the orb to be something wonderful.  Later, it’s Jack who claims responsibility for bringing it back, and Teal’c tries to take a share at that point too.  Sam just stays focused on finding a solution rather than dwelling on whose fault it is that it’s happened – good on her!

 

Then we get to see the really big door!  Whenever ‘wildfire’ is activated and the base is sealed off, I always wonder – where in the mountain is that door anyway?  It really is huge, wherever it is!

 

Sam and Janet get one of their high-powered “we are clever women” scenes, where they talk in polysyllables and bounce ideas off each other.  I always think it’s really fun when they do this, and they make a very good team.

 

I love the way Lt Simmons orders up the UV units.  He’s obviously not used to giving orders, judging by the way he picks up the phone rather nervously and says, “Let’s get some UV units in the gate room”.  It’s very sweet!

 

And when the UV units arrive, we get the revelation of glowy blue alien goo that is spreading and can eat through solid concrete – ooh, scary!!

 

Continuity error: when Sam gets in the elevator, the numbers click up from 21 to 22 and then the elevator stops.  Sam bangs on the door and, when Daniel comes to get her out, the numbers on the door say 28!

 

Sam is put in rather an awkward position here, having to deal with all the idiot men who seemingly know nothing and are always doing everything wrong.  You can see the tiniest bit of frustration starting to appear when she outlines the idea about lowering the oxygen levels.  I always wonder why they can’t issue oxygen masks all round and then just suck all the oxygen out of the base, but then maybe they don’t have enough tanks to cover all the personnel.

 

Don’t you just love shows that have lines like – “Then in just under three hours, the organism will be fed enough energy to spread across the face of the Earth!”  The build-up is great – first it was just Jack pinned to wall that they had to solve, then came the time limit set by Lt Simmons being allergic to the antidote, and then suddenly the entire planet is at risk.  They never do things by halves on this show!

 

In the scene where Daniel figures out the orb is trying to communicate, there is clear evidence that he’s been ‘working round the clock’.  This backs up his comment in the Season Three ep Crystal Skull, where he complains about Sam and Jack giving up for the night.  Poor Daniel!

 

You can understand Hammond’s exasperation when Sam says to increase the oxygen levels – scientists, eh?  First they say yes, then they say no – there’s just no reasoning with them, is there, George?  It must be hard for him to take responsibility for decisions based on theories he doesn’t really understand, though obviously he makes sure everything is explained carefully before he decides what to do.  Who’d be a general?  Certainly not me!

 

Teal’c’s role in the episode has been that of loyal companion.  Admittedly, there isn’t much else he can contribute, while Sam and Daniel have been working their asses off looking for a solution, but they haven’t been to see Jack once and, when they arrive to implement their latest plan, they pretty much ignore him.  Sam does step up to tell him that what they’re about to try might well kill him, so we get a touching hand-squeeze moment, but that’s about it.  And besides, as far as she was concerned, Jack was unconscious, so she wasn’t exactly giving him much choice in the matter!

 

I love the fact that the first words spoken by the aliens are “Good morning, campers”.  It’s really funny, but at the same time makes it even more chilling, that they are using Jack to communicate.  I’ve always found it rather disturbing in TV shows when main characters are possessed.

 

There’s a wonderful, wonderful moment in this scene – so subtle that I totally missed it until it was pointed out to me.  When the aliens say that Jack wants to live, they look at Daniel, referencing the film when it was Daniel who taught Jack the value of life again.  Then, again, when they are given the option of going to the primordial world, they look at Daniel, using Jack’s knowledge of his character to decide that he is trustworthy.  Teal’c is a staunch friend, but it’s Jack’s relationship with Daniel that convinces the aliens to trust them.

 

It’s a good job they have state-of-the-art computers at the SGC.  They’ve only got two minutes before the base explodes when the system shuts down.  It takes my office PC 15 minutes to boot up in the morning, by which time the planet would be doomed five times over!

 

However, even after the end of the episode, we’re still not safe!  When Jack pushes the orb through the open wormhole, there’s still a patch of glowy goo on his shoulder, so some of the aliens must have stayed behind!!

 

It’s really nice that Jack makes reference to Teal’c’s joke in the final moments, acknowledging what they’ve shared in the course of the episode.

 

Altogether not bad, and with some great moments.  Lots of team work and exploration of character relationships.

 

Best Bits

 

Daniel’s mouth encounters his foot:

Daniel: He gets nervous like that every time he’s around you.  Maybe a crush!

Simmons: We’re recording now.

Daniel: Oops! Hey – the artifact!

Daniel always seems to put his foot in it with Sam and other guys – he walks in on her kissing Narim and later interrupts the handholding with Martouf too.

 

Male bonding in extreme circumstances:

Jack: You don’t have to stick around.

Teal’c: Undomesticated equines could not remove me.

Jack: Wild horses, Teal’c.  It’s w-  That’s a joke!  You told a joke.  Don’t make me laugh.

Teal’c: Very well.

Jack: You’re a good man, my friend.

Teal’c: As are you, my friend.

This is just a wonderful scene.
