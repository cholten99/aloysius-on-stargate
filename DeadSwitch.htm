Deadman Switch

 

 

Writer: Robert C Cooper

Director: Martin Wood

 

Ah, trees!  Jack’s favourite.  This is a cross between the opening of Prisoners and the opening of One False Step – trudging through trees and moss to retrieve a downed UAV.  Jack must be having the time of his life!  It does seem a bit of a waste of the flagship first contact team, even if they do think it was shot down by the Goa’uld.

 

The shield trap is pretty cool, though why Daniel has to find it by walking face first into it is beyond me – it’s even worse than him tripping over the dead Linvris at the start of Legacy.

 

And so we meet Arris Boch, possibly the coolest one-off character ever to appear in the show.  He’s just great – he makes a fantastic adversary and an even better almost-ally.  His antagonistic wit rivals even that of Jack, so the two of them together is very funny.  He’s also got a lot of interesting stuff we’ve never seen before – the one-way shield, the funky voice-altering helmet, the torpedo-shaped gun, and the zat-proof armour.  Boba Fett, step aside – there’s a new and far cooler bounty hunter in town!

 

However, Teal’c does an excellent job of competing for the title of cool when he fails to even flinch at Boch’s weapon blast destroying the tree branch right behind him.

 

I just love the idea that SG-1 are notorious throughout the galaxy for killing System Lords; it’s pretty believable and a great premise for an episode.

 

The fact that Boch finds Jack’s attempt to throw a knife through the shield so amusing is fair enough – if Daniel can’t walk through it, it’s a fairly safe bet that a knife can’t be thrown through…

 

Boch’s ego is huge, but he displays it in such a cool way.  It makes a great change from the cliched posturing of the Goa’uld.  He automatically assumes they might have heard of him, but the realisation that they don’t most likely lessens his opinion of them rather than knocking his opinion of himself.

 

When Boch says the password and the door to his cloaked ship opens, Jack leaps backwards and grabs onto Teal’c with a yelp of surprise.  Teal’c and Jack then exchange glances that clearly say – “Did you just yelp and grab onto me?” and “Did I just yelp and grab onto you?” – very, very funny.

 

The whole scene in the cell of the cargo ship is great.  Boch and SG-1 bounce off each other very well.  Having a baddie with a sense of humour makes all the difference.  Daniel is so funny when he asks how much they’re all worth.  Poor little honey pie is so disappointed when Boch says he’s only worth a day’s rations.  He may say that it doesn’t make him feel any better when he finds out Boch is lying, but his expression in this scene tells a different story!

 

The exchanging of glances between all of SG-1 throughout this episode is wonderful.  We’ve reached a stage in the show where they know each other and we know them well enough for the silent communication to work brilliantly.

 

Boch says he likes Jack because he’s quick – obviously the rest of the galaxy aren’t fooled by Jack’s dumbwitted act, which is understandable since they know he’s the leader of the best of the Tau’ri – not a position you can reach by being stupid.

 

As Boch herds Jack and Daniel up the hill on their first excursion, Jack is limping slightly and wincing, and the knees of his pants are covered in mud – little accident on an earlier take perhaps?

 

Interesting role reversal when Jack shoots Boch – Daniel turns into Mr Negative, pointing out that they’re trapped on the planet, while it’s Jack who thinks outside the box and suggests flying to a closer planet with a Stargate.

 

Of course, at this stage, we’re only 15 minutes into the episode, and Boch has already shown remarkable foresight and pre-emptive tactics, so it’s no surprise to discover that, yet again, he’s several steps ahead.  I love the fact that, instead of just revealing his armour is zat-proof right away, he allows them to think they’ve defeated him, so he can see what they do, and enjoy the later revelation even more.

 

Why Daniel decides to jump through a tree on the way down into the clearing is beyond me, and Jack doesn’t look too pleased when it swings round and hits him in the face!  Then we get classic Jack/Daniel banter as they try to get into the ship – not quite as good at the fight in One False Step, but the best you’re going to get without alien influences.  They both look very sexy in this scene too, with their individualised ways of wearing their identical uniforms.

 

Knowing that Boch is totally in control of the situation rather decreases the tension of Jack and Daniel trying to get Sam and Teal’c out of the cell while the countdown is going on.  It’s still a great scene, though, and when Jack tells Daniel to go and find the crystal, it’s totally obvious he knows Daniel will never have enough time to retrieve it before the ship blows.  He’s just trying to get him to safety, and knows he won’t just leave, so he invents a legitimate reason for Daniel not to be there.

 

Jack loses patience with Boch’s games very quickly, although he refuses to give up, shooting him again with the zat, just in case.  The way Daniel apologises and asks if the deal is still on the table is absolutely adorable – and Boch’s amusement at Jack’s exasperation and Daniel’s attempts at sweet-talking pretty much match those of the viewer.  It’s great to have a character looking at SG-1 from our point of view.

 

Coming up against someone he can’t intimidate, antagonise, bait or out-think must be really frustrating for Jack.  None of his usual tactics work, and Boch paints him into a corner at every turn.  He still has time to admire the zooming Goa’uld-voice goggles when Boch lends them to him, though – never can resist playing with new toys!

 

Poor old Daniel doesn’t get to contribute much to the assault on the cave – he hasn’t got a weapon, so I’m not sure what he was hoping to achieve by being there.  Jack and Teal’c work wonderfully well together, though, executing a very solid plan without even discussing it first.  That silent communication thing is really good!

 

And so the plot thickens – the Goa’uld is really a Tok’ra, and Boch’s forward-thinking strikes again – keeping Sam behind so she can’t vouch for Korah is very clever.

 

Sam’s not the type to sit idly by and waste time alone with their captor – she’s obviously been taking lessons from Daniel.  She starts off by trying to get a handle on Boch’s technology, but soon turns to negotiating, trying first to appeal to his sense of duty to humankind (which he doesn’t have, of course) and then figuring out what would be most likely to persuade him – the offer to help rescue his son.  Unfortunately, since that story isn’t actually true, her plan fails, but it was a nice try!  We get to see quite a lot of good ideas from SG-1 in this ep – they just don’t seem all that great because none of them work.

 

In the end, Sam does actually find the only thing that can push Boch’s buttons, suggesting that his amorality is really just a façade and that, underneath the arrogance and ruthlessness, he really does care about the fate of humans.  She also figures out that he’s lying about the blue stuff he puts in his water – full marks for observation and perception on this one, Sam!

 

Boch certainly doesn’t care about the environment.  He casually tosses aside the plastic containers once he empties them of the drug, thus littering the planet’s lovely unspoiled forest with non-degradable refuse – bad Arris!

 

Boch’s change of heart is a little swift for my tastes – I realise he hates the Goa’uld, but his years of unscrupulous bargaining are overturned remarkably quickly.  I’m sure he must have come across Tok’ra before and not been swayed.  I guess the influence of SG-1 is just too strong for him to resist.

 

Teal’c gets to do his honourable sacrifice bit – he always love the opportunity to do that.  He even has the grace to praise Boch for letting the others go – what a guy!

 

I like the way the Tok’ra’s injuries gradually become less and less obvious without it actually being pointed out – subtle reminder of a symbiote’s healing powers.

 

This is a really great episode – all the team contribute something, and their interaction with Arris Boch is just brilliant.  I’ve always been disappointed that this storyline is never revisited, with Sam and Janet figuring out how to cure the rashna addiction.  I’d love Boch to come back into the show for another episode – another good fanfiction subject, and there are some good Arris Boch stories out there.

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Jack has a customary fit of malapropism:

Jack: So, what exactly did you have in mind regarding the capture of this Goa’uld – what was his name, Kevlar?

Boch: Keltar.

 

 

Best Bits

 

I’d like to transcribe the whole episode here, because it’s all great – but I don’t have time, so these few scenes will have to do!

 

A rare example of Jack actually asking Sam to explain something further:

Sam: Sir, he’s not Goa’uld.

Jack: And? But? So? Therefore?

 

Medical assumptions and pedantic prisoners:

Boch: Dr Jackson, if you wouldn’t mind – treating my wound.

Daniel: I’m an archaeologist.

Boch: I know – but you’re also a doctor.

Daniel: Of archaeology.

Boch: Never mind.  Captain!  You must have some medical training.

Sam: Actually, I’m a Major now.

Boch: Oh!  Well, how very important.  I’ll inform the galaxy.

 

Boch’s arrogance meets Jack’s sarcasm:

Sam: How do you keep from getting killed?

Boch: It takes talent.

Jack: So, how do you keep from getting killed?

 

Daniel takes a turn for the mercenary:

Daniel: So, what do you expect to get for us?

Jack: Daniel?

Daniel: Just curious.

Boch: Well, Teal’c is worth the most.  The System Lords would love to make a good example of him.  And Carter here – well, she has the memories of the Tok’ra Jolinar.  And you, O’Neill, well, you’re considered a pain in the mikta.

Jack: Neck?

Teal’c: No.

Daniel: What about – what about me?

Boch: You?  I’ll be happy to get a day’s rations for you.

Daniel: Oh!  A day’s rations.

Jack: Trade ya.

 

Boch’s arrogance meets Jack’s sarcasm again:

Boch: Do you want to know how I became the greatest bounty hunter in the galaxy?

Jack: More than life itself.

 

Brains and negativity do an unusual switch:

Daniel: Jack, he disabled the DHD and Sam said we can’t fly that ship all the way home.

Jack: So, we’ll fly to a closer planet with a Stargate.

Daniel: Good thinking!

Jack: It happens.

 

Classic Jack/Daniel banter:

Daniel: Well, this is the place.

Jack: Yeah.  How d’you open that door?

Daniel: Uh, it opened when he said that word.

Jack: Voice activated.  What was the word?

Daniel: Uh… ‘bark’ something.

Jack: Um… omenbach, bark-lounger, Beetlejuice!

Daniel: Barkna – it was barkna.

Jack: Well, if it was ‘barkna’, the door would be opening, wouldn’t it?

Daniel: This was your idea.

Jack: You’re the linguist!

Daniel: Well, I don’t remember.

Jack: Well, try!

Daniel: Okay – I said “can we talk about this”, he said “inside”…

Jack: I said “inside what”, he said –

Daniel: He said “barokna”!

[The door opens.]

Jack: Gesundheit.

 

Daniel’s worth is greater than he thought:

Boch: Oh, uh, Dr Jackson – I was lying to you before.  The price on your head is much more than a day’s rations.  Actually, the Goa’uld want to see you dead as much as anyone, for figuring out the Stargate.

Daniel: Thank you – that doesn’t make me feel any better.
