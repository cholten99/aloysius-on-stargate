The Enemy Within

 

Writer: Brad Wright

Director: Dennis Barry

 

This episode opens with banter between Jack and Kawalsky.  The jokey relationship Jack shares with Kawalsky is later replaced by banter with Daniel, but it has a very different style.  Jack and Kawalsky take on the roles of naughty schoolboys messing about in class and exchanging glances behind the back of the teacher, in this case Hammond.  The banter between Jack and Daniel is more like friends who play on the differences between them to wind each other up.

 

When the Goa’uld start attacking the gate, the contrast between Jack and Daniel is once again made evident; Daniel’s naivety versus Jack’s cynicism with regard to what the Goa’uld are doing.

 

Daniel: You don’t think the Goa’uld are sending people through, do you?

Jack: It’d be like bugs on a windshield.

 

Jack’s tone here has a hint of relish to it, showing his pleasure at the thought of his enemies suffering, while Daniel’s horror at the prospect demonstrates his amazing ability to show compassion for almost anyone.

 

This episode has two main threads – Teal’c proving his worth, and Kawalsky being infested.  They are tied together by the presence of Colonel Kennedy, who introduces the SGC/Pentagon conflict with his unwelcome attitude to both issues.  Jack’s feelings are extremely strong in these areas and this is clearly shown by his reaction to the news that the Pentagon want to take Teal’c away for tests.  As usual, Jack is far from hesitant in making his true feelings known, causing Hammond to give him one of many, many warnings about his choice of words!  Jack not only disapproves of the idea on principle, but also feels responsible for Teal’c’s welfare, as his sponsor on Earth.  I get the feeling Hammond is gradually becoming aware by now that Jack isn’t going to be the easiest of subordinates to deal with!

 

There may be many times when Jack says things that Teal’c doesn’t get, but the scene where Jack goes to see him in his cell clearly demonstrates that there is a basic understanding between them.  Teal’c knows what it’s like to be a soldier forced to follow unpalatable orders, so Jack does not need to explain his position and Teal’c does not blame him for what his superiors want to do.  This episode is very good for showing the similarities and strong relationship between Jack and Teal’c.

 

It does the same for Sam and Daniel, who are presented as already having a very comfortable working relationship.  They make an excellent teaching team when explaining the use of the DHD, working well with each other.  There’s also a nice touch in that Sam draws the cube diagram used by Daniel in the movie to explain how the Stargate identifies a point in space.

 

Jack’s behaviour during Teal’c’s interview with Kennedy is priceless.  He is deliberately as annoying as possible, taking every opportunity to hammer home the point that Teal’c is a person, with the same rights as anyone else.  I love the way he spells out Teal’c’s name to the woman taking the minutes – the perfect way to emphasise the importance of Teal’c’s identity as an individual.

 

We get another cute moment between Sam and Daniel where Sam demonstrates concern for Daniel’s health, asking him when he last slept.  Daniel later returns the favour by going to check on Sam, after she has been injured by the Goa’uld possessing Kawalsky.  Of course, as soon as poor Daniel tries to take a nap, his purpose is thwarted by the discovery of a dead body – things very rarely go right for Daniel!

 

Jack is everywhere in this episode, constantly watching over all his friends:

He barges into Teal’c’s interview, he confronts Kawalsky in the control room when Sam is taken hostage, he is there to open the elevator doors to rescue Sam, he gives Kawalsky moral support in the infirmary and is the one to tell him that he’s been infested, he follows Kennedy to monitor him when he goes to check on Teal’c’s symbiote, and then he’s back in the infirmary again when they talk to the Goa’uld.  In fact, Jack casts himself as the champion of the underdog in this episode.  He acts as liaison between Teal’c, Kawalsky and the senior officers, making sure they are represented properly in all discussions and that they always have someone in their corner.

 

Brief aside: couldn’t the writers have thought of a different name either for Kawalsky or Jack’s son?  There are plenty of names out there and it seems odd for them both to be called Charlie.

 

The scene where Hammond talks to the symbiote establishes several important things about the Goa’uld.  In case we hadn’t already figured it out from Apophis in the pilot, we discover that they are evil through and through, arrogant and unreasonable, totally unwilling to negotiate, super humanly strong and thoroughly unpleasant!

 

Kawalsky’s rapid changes from the misery and fear of an infested soldier to the superior confidence of the Goa’uld are very well portrayed.

 

The SGC personnel present a completely united front against Kennedy when discussing whether to proceed with the operation.  Kennedy faces a totally hostile room, with nobody willing to entertain his ideas even for a moment.  Hammond and SG-1 are firm in their opinion that even the remotest chance of saving Kawalsky is far more important than gaining information about their enemy.

 

Jack’s refusal to accept that Kawalsky might die tells us several things about his character.  His “nobody gets left behind” attitude is fuelled by his genuine concern for anybody under his command.  The loss of a friend and colleague is not something he wants to contemplate.  However, the scene in the infirmary just before the operation also shows Jack’s inability to express his feelings.  He is clearly very uncomfortable with Kawalsky’s show of emotion and tries to diffuse the situation by resorting to humour, as usual.

 

I really like Dr Warner – he deals with Hammond’s brusque manner very well and is obviously genuinely delighted when it looks as though Kawalsky is okay.

 

The apparent success of the operation is not the end Jack’s work for the day, though.  When Sam comes in to tell him that Kennedy has orders to take Teal’c away, he is straight out the door to go and argue about it.  He certainly isn’t one to let things go when he feels strongly about them – he will just keep on arguing his point until the opposition capitulates.

 

It’s really sad when Teal’c goes to see Kawalsky after the operation.  He looks so delighted when Kawalsky calls him “my friend” and then, of course we find out that it was just the symbiote’s way of tricking the soldiers into leaving them alone.  I’m not sure I entirely understand Goa’uld physiology and the way the possession works – the whole melding and dead husk explanation seems rather confusing, but I’m happy just to go with it and not worry!

 

The symbiote isn’t very thorough – it fails to kill Dr Warner in the MRI room earlier on and now fails to confirm the kill when it attacks Teal’c.  Seems rather sloppy to me, but I guess it has the excuse of only being a baby Goa’uld!

 

One of my favourite Teal’c moments is when Kawalsky enters the gate room and Teal’c is revealed standing in his path on the ramp.  He presents a formidable opponent and the way he says, “You cannot pass” is brilliant.  However, the moment is ruined somewhat by the bizarre roar Kawalsky emits before he rushes Teal’c – it always make me laugh, it sounds so silly.

 

All I have to say about the removal of the back of Kawalsky’s head by the disengaging wormhole is – ewwwwww!

 

Teal’c has his first guilt trip here – killing a Goa’uld who has infested someone his friends care about.  Poor Teal’c has a tough time in this respect, although Jack doesn’t blame him in the least, showing impressive understanding in the circumstances.

 

And so our team is finally established and ready to set out on their adventures.  The pilot ended with them all lined up in front of the gate as a sign of things to come, and this episode ends with them all lined up in front of the gate officially as SG-1, about to embark on their first mission.

 

This isn’t one of my favourite episodes.  I always think it’s a shame that we lose Kawalsky so early, as I really like him as a character.  Also, the story is confined entirely to the base, and there’s a huge amount of exposition thrown in.  However, it serves a good purpose – bringing Teal’c properly into the team and giving us important information about the Goa’uld.  It also has lots of nice team bonding moments as the four main characters get to know each other and establish themselves within the group dynamic.

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Jack’s catchphrase crops up again when he’s talking to Kawalsky in the infirmary:

Jack: Oh, for crying out loud, it’s not your last conversation!

 

We also get the first instance of a reference to the Wizard of Oz, when Kawalsky comes round after the operation:

Jack: That’s right, Dorothy, it was all a dream!

 

Best Bits

 

Jack being his usual, direct self on arrival in the briefing room:

Jack: Permission to barge in, sir?

 

The eventual put down of Colonel Kennedy by the President himself, no less:

Hammond: (chortling down the phone) Aw, hell, sir – he’s right here.  You can tell him yourself!
