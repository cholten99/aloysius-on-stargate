A Matter of Time

 

 

This episode is primarily a Jack characterisation episode.  It gives us a greater insight into his past and the more serious side of his personality.  First of all, he has to experience the loss of a team commander whom he trained and recommended for the position.  He reacts with characteristic eagerness to launch a rescue mission without wanting to wait for more information.  However, we also get to see a rare demonstration of Jack’s intelligence, as in Singularity, since his expertise in astronomy gives him knowledge of the properties of black holes.

 

Then, later, we get the interaction with Colonel Cromwell, which not only explains Jack’s reference to being in prison before in Prisoners, but also gives us the background to his vehement “nobody gets left behind” attitude.  The way Jack’s previous relationship to Cromwell is gradually revealed is very effective.  Jack is immediately hostile to him when he appears, which is unusual enough to make us sit up and take notice, giving an extra dimension of mystery to an already tense episode.

 

The contrast between Jack and Sam is also brought out in this episode.  Sam’s enthusiasm for science gets the better of her when she wants to record the progress of the black hole and Jack’s response to that is a very strong one: “We are witnessing good men dying in slow motion, Captain!”  Ordinarily on the show, we have Daniel presenting the humanitarian point of view in opposition to Jack’s military attitude, but here Jack has the emotional argument versus Sam’s analytical scientific approach.

 

I do find the techno-babble slightly overwhelming at times in this episode, particularly when they are dealing with the time paradoxes and relativity.  As usual, though, Jack gives an outlet to possible audience frustration with this, by calling a halt to Sam’s increasingly technical outpourings.

 

One of the best things about this episode is the array of peripheral characters who are involved.  It’s nice to see Lt Simmons at the gate controls for a change at the beginning and the use of Colonel Cromwell adds another level to the plot.  Dr Frasier and Sgt Siler both get screen time, giving greater variety to an episode which is essentially confined to Cheyenne Mountain.  Also, we get the first introduction of Major Davies as the Pentagon liaison, a great character who reappears in several episodes throughout the next few seasons.
