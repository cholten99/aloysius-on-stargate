The Fifth Race

 

 

This is a great episode for characterisation and the interaction of the team.  It starts off with Daniel in lecture mode, getting all excited about something he’s discovered, and encountering either bemused indulgence or impatient frustration from the other characters.  The relationship between Daniel and the rest of the team is demonstrated very clearly here.  He tells them that the writing from the MALP telemetry matches one of the scripts from Ernest’s planet and then just stops, expecting the others to recognise the significance.  Then follows a great sequence of close-ups on the others as they exchange confused or bemused glances before he goes on to explain it to them.  Of course, as soon as he makes his ideas clear, the other three immediately leap to support him and recommend the mission.

 

Their reactions to the offworld location are classic.  Jack sees an empty room and gives up on the place almost immediately.  Sam points out that something must be providing the power and wants to know how the technology works.  Daniel doesn’t want to give up on something he feels could be very important, and tries the age-old method of calling out in an attempt to make contact.

 

Similarly, the contrast in attitudes between Daniel and Sam is highlighted in their response to Jack’s newfound knowledge.  Daniel is fascinated by the whole thing and waffles excitedly about alien meeting places and alliances, while Sam joins General Hammond in his concerns about security.  She sums up the military viewpoint very well: “The point is, sir, that Colonel O’Neill is unwillingly speaking an alien language.”  This also shows why I love sci-fi shows so much – nowhere else could you find such wonderfully ridiculous lines that make perfect sense!

 

The loss of speech allows Richard Dean Anderson to show off his acting skills, as he brings into play a tremendous range of facial expressions and body language, very effectively demonstrating Jack’s confusion, frustration and sense of helplessness.

 

The situation of Jack having greater knowledge than Sam and Daniel is great fun.  Instead of getting impatient with them because he can’t understand their ideas, he gets impatient with them because they can’t understand his – great role reversal.  It’s also interesting the way Sam and Daniel react to not being the foremost experts in their fields any more.  Naturally, they find it very disconcerting, but Daniel is better at accepting Jack’s sudden gain in knowledge than Sam, who seems the smallest bit resentful of it.  The fact that it’s Jack who has suddenly surpassed them makes it all the more entertaining.

 

The introduction of the Asgard is really cool, and Jack rises to the challenge of first contact admirably.  “We may not be ready for a lot of this stuff, but you folks should understand, we’re out there now.”  I expect the Asgard are impressed by his confidence and directness, which is probably why Thor chooses him as the representative for Earth in Fair Game.

 

The only problem I have with this episode is that the tension of Sam’s and Teal’c’s predicament on the hot planet is not very well maintained.  We are only connected to them via Sam’s transmission and this dilutes the atmosphere somewhat.

 

The episode as a whole is all about the bonds between the members of SG-1: Sam and Teal’c going offworld to try and find a way to help Jack; Daniel refusing to leave Jack without an interpreter; Jack overcoming his pain to use the knowledge to save Sam and Teal’c; Teal’c offering to accompany Jack to the unknown gate address.  They all demonstrate absolute faith in the abilities and intentions of the others when faced with unknown dangers and confusing situations.

 

 

Best Bits

 

Jack’s response to discovering that his vocabulary has been tampered with:

Jack: Doesn’t anybody think this is odd?

 

The little self-deprecating shrug Jack gives Sam when she asks who figured out how to fix the DHD and get her and Teal’c home – it’s so cute!
