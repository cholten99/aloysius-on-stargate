An Elven Tale: The Return Of The King


Six horses set out from Helm’s Deep on the morning of the victory against Saruman’s army, travelling a different road to the train of people setting out after them. The people pointed their footsteps towards Edoras and home, while the horses made for Isengard, and perhaps further danger.


Theoden-King, with his aide Gamling and his nephew Eomer, rode to determine the fate of his enemy, accompanied by a wizard, a ranger, a dwarf, and myself, an elf, all of whom had already travelled far in the pursuit of victory. A battle had been won but, as Gandalf had pointed out, the war was far from over.


Arod still gamely carried both myself and Gimli, covering the ground with long strides, apparently untroubled by his double burden. We made good speed, but even horses of Rohan cannot gallop indefinitely and we were forced to a walking pace at times to preserve their strength. It was during one of these rest periods that Gimli broke the silence between us.


“I am sorry for your loss, Legolas,” he said, his voice gruff.


I was glad I had my back to him as the pain of Haldir’s death washed over me anew. I sighed, trying to find some balance in the murky waters of my grief.


“Haldir knew centuries of peace and freedom,” I replied. “He gave his life so that others would have the chance to experience the same. I do not believe he would begrudge them his sacrifice.”


“And what about you?” Gimli persisted. “How are you holding up?”


“He was my friend – and sometimes more than that.” My gaze was drawn involuntarily towards Aragorn, riding ahead of us. “But I could not give him what he wanted of me, and for that I am sorry.”


We lapsed into silence again, and I was grateful that Gimli let the subject drop, though I was also warmed by his concern. I would have time to grieve for Haldir properly later, when all this was over. I only hoped that no more of my friends would be added to those I would mourn.


It was a sombre procession that walked beneath the trees of Fangorn shortly after the sun had reached its zenith. I think all of us were affected by the atmosphere of the forest. Mist swirled around the horses’ feet as they picked their way between the gnarled trunks, finding a twisting path among boulders and shrubs. I felt the power of the trees as a more immediate force now; they had awoken and taken a stand against the rising evil. We were certainly being watched and appraised, but evidently our purpose was accepted as we were not impeded in any way.


It was as we left the forest that the unexpected sound of laughter reached my ears from up ahead. We came out onto a flooded plain with the Tower of Orthanc rising up at its centre and the surrounded wall shattered and ruined. There, atop the wall, as if they had nary a care in the world, sat two figures who brought a glimmer of joy to my darkened heart. Merry and Pippin grinned down at us as we all brought our horses to a halt.


Merry pushed himself, somewhat unsteadily, to his feet, gestured at the tower behind him, and cried, “Welcome, my lords – to Isengard!”


At my back, Gimli harrumphed. “You young rascals!” he called back, though I could tell he was just as delighted as I to see the hobbits alive and well. “A merry hunt you’ve led us on – and now we find you feasting and – and smoking!”


For indeed, the two hobbits had laid out a veritable picnic atop the wall; both had lit pipes and were clutching tankards.


Aragorn twisted in his saddle and looked back at me with a broad grin, and I could not help returning it. It seemed that no matter what happened, hobbits would always survive to celebrate afterwards.


Pippin responded to Gimli, his words more than a little slurred. “We are sitting on a field of victory, enjoying a few well-earned comforts.” He took a bite of his sandwich and continued to speak around the mouthful. “The salted pork is particularly good.”


“Salted pork?” Gimli spluttered in combined astonishment and envy.


Gandalf muttered, “Hobbits,” under his breath, but I could see he was suppressing a grin of his own.


Merry gesticulated with his pipe, clearly very pleased with himself. “We’re under orders from Treebeard, who’s taken over management of Isengard.”


It seemed that we needn’t have worried about what Saruman might be up to; the Ents and the hobbits had done a fine job of demolishing his power base, as Merry and Pippin took delight in telling us. Aragorn swung Pippin up behind him, while Merry took up position behind Eomer and we made our way to the base of the tower, the two hobbits regaling us with the remarkable tale of the siege of Isengard. Once again, the small folk had surpassed expectations and achieved something astonishing; I resolved never again to be surprised at their exploits.


The horses picked their way carefully through the debris and fetlock-deep water. There were huge treelike creatures I knew well to be Ents moving around the area, and one approached us as we reached the tower. I took him to be Treebeard, the Ent the hobbits had mentioned earlier, and he addressed us in a deep and breathy voice.


“Young Master Gandalf,” he said. “I’m glad you’ve come.” The fact that he referred to Gandalf as “young” was not wasted on me; I was already aware how ancient the Ents were. “Wood and water, stock and stone, I can master. But there’s a wizard to manage here… locked in his tower.”


“Show yourself.” Aragorn spoke softly, evidently directing his words at Saruman, though I was not sure how he expected the wizard to hear him.


“Be careful,” Gandalf warned. “Even in defeat, Saruman is dangerous.”


Gimli spoke up, in his usual manner. “Well, let’s just have his head and be done with it.”


“No,” Gandalf replied. “We need him alive.” He elaborated, at a grim look from Eomer. “We need him to talk.”


As if summoned by our discussion of him (and perhaps this was actually the case), a voice echoed down from above.


“You have fought many wars and slain many men, Theoden-King, and made peace afterwards.” I looked up to see an old man in a white robe stepping forwards to the edge of the platform atop the tower. His tone was reasonable, his words placatory. “Can we not take counsel together as we once did, my old friend?” He leaned on a slim, black staff, looking frail and weary. “Can we not have peace, you and I?”


“We shall have peace,” Theoden called back. Aragorn looked across at him in surprise, but then the king continued, his tone growing more angry with every word. “We shall have peace when you answer for the burning of the Westfold, and the children that lie dead there! We shall have peace when the lives of the soldiers, whose bodies were hewn even as they lay dead against the gates of the Hornburg, are avenged! When you hang from a gibbet for the sport of your own crows – we shall have peace.”


Aragorn turned his gaze back up to Saruman. I wondered if he shared my thoughts that such an approach was unlikely to put Saruman into the right frame of mind to give us information. My fears were confirmed as the wizard responded with contempt and anger of his own.


“Gibbets and crows? Dotard!” He turned his attention to Gandalf, though he did not attempt to wheedle his way into his fellow wizard’s good graces. “What do you want here, Gandalf Greyhame? Let me guess. The key of Orthanc? Or perhaps the keys to Barad-dur itself? Along with the crowns of the Seven Kings and the rods of the Five Wizards!”


Gandalf remained calm in the face of this tirade, presumably trying to prevent the conversation from descending into a pointless exchange of insults and accusations.


“Your treachery has cost many lives,” he said, his words quiet, but still able to carry to their recipient. “Thousands more are now at risk. But you could save them Saruman. You were deep in the enemy’s counsel.”


“So you have come here for information,” Saruman surmised. “I have some for you.” He produced a large black sphere from within his robes, its surface flecked with gold. “Something festers in the heart of Middle Earth. Something that you have failed to see.” His tone shifted from contempt to awe. “But the Great Eye has seen it. Even now he presses his advantage. His attack will come soon.”


Gandalf urged Shadowfax closer to the base of the tower, as if this would somehow make Saruman’s meaning clearer.


“You’re all going to die,” our enemy continued, presumably trying to frighten us with his threats. It seemed to be working on Merry, who glanced around at us, his expression fearful. “But you know this, don’t you Gandalf? You cannot think this Ranger will ever sit upon the throne of Gondor. This exile, crept from the shadows, will never be crowned king. Gandalf does not hesitate to sacrifice those closest to him, those he professes to love.”


I wondered what Saruman hoped to achieve in criticising Gandalf, who had ever been our steadfast ally. I reflected on our journey so far, and noted that the only one Gandalf had thus far sacrificed had been himself, down in the mines.


But the other wizard had not yet finished. “Tell me, what words of comfort did you give the Halfling before you sent him to his doom?” Gandalf lowered his eyes at this, his expression pained, though I do not believe any of the rest of us blamed him for Frodo’s involvement in our quest. “The path you have set him on can only lead to death.”


Gimli reached the end of his patience at this point. “I’ve heard enough!” he declared. He nudged me. “Shoot him. Stick an arrow in his gob.”


I was not of a mind to disagree with him, annoyed as I was at our enemy’s attempt to discourage us. I reached over my shoulder to pull an arrow from my quiver, but Gandalf halted my action with an outstretched hand and a simple command.


“Come down, Saruman,” he called to his rival, “and your life will be spared.”


This appeal fell on completely deaf ears. “Save your pity and your mercy. I have no use for it!” Saruman cried.


With that, he raised his staff above his head and a torrent of fire shot from the end of it, streaming to the ground and completely engulfing both Gandalf and Shadowfax. All the other horses skittered backwards in alarm and we were forced to raise our arms to shield our faces from the heat. Shock radiated from everyone in the group, but the flames quickly receded to reveal wizard and horse untouched.


Completely unfazed by the attack, Gandalf stated matter-of-factly, “Saruman, your staff is broken.”


The other wizard looked at the rod in his hand and it exploded into shards. Saruman staggered slightly and clutched his burned hand to his chest. Meanwhile, another figure appeared on the platform behind him. It was Wormtongue, his black clothes and hair making a marked contrast to the entirely white attire of his master.


Theoden spotted him and spoke. “Grima, you need not follow him. You were not always as you are now. You were once a man of Rohan. Come down.”


With my keen eyes, I saw Wormtongue bow slightly and mouth “My Lord”, though he did not speak aloud.


Given the state in which we found Theoden at Edoras, and given Wormtongue's part in that, I was impressed with Theoden's compassion. I sensed that Wormtongue desperately needed an authority figure to tell him what to do, and that he realised Saruman's control over the situation was failing.


Unfortunately, Saruman's grasp of things was not so perceptive. He poured scorn on the exchange. “A man of Rohan? What is the house of Rohan but a thatched barn where brigands drink in the reek and their brats roll on the floor with the dogs? The victory at Helm’s Deep does not belong to you, Theoden Horse-Master. You are a lesser son of greater sires.”


Making a visible effort to ignore the barbs of the wizard, Theoden continued in his attempt to bring Wormtongue round to our side. “Grima, come down. Be free of him.”


“Free?” Saruman spat. “He will never be free.”


Wormtongue spoke for the first time, simply saying, “No.”


I wasn't sure if he was agreeing with Saruman's pronouncement, or if he was trying to defy it, but he didn't get a chance to elaborate, since Saruman shouted, “Get down, you cur!” and struck Grima, who fell to the ground with a shocked cry.


Gandalf completely ignored the Wormtongue issue, repeating his previous statement, presumably in an attempt to get the conversation back onto what he considered a more important topic. “Saruman. You were deep in the enemy's counsel. Tell us what you know!”


Saruman apparently decided it was time to deal. “You withdraw your guard, and I will tell you where your doom will be decided. I will not be held prisoner here.”


I was certain that Gandalf would not trust him but Wormtongue took matters into his own hands. He rose up, grabbed Saruman from behind and stabbed him twice in the back with a knife he had evidently been concealing. My arrow was loosed as he moved, but it was too late to save Saruman. Wormtonge staggered backwards and fell, the shaft protruding from his chest. Saruman turned slowly, his expression radiating shock at what his erstwhile servant had done to him, and toppled off the tower. His body spun as it fell, tracked by the eyes of everyone who waited on the ground, and impaled itself on the spikes of a big water wheel in front of us. Merry and Pippin gasped in horror.


Gandalf's words to Theoden were urgent. “Send word to all our allies and to every corner of Middle Earth that still stnads free. The enemy moves against us. We need to know where he will strike.”


As he stopped speaking, the wheel groaned and began to move, slowly submerging Saruman's body in the murky water. Something slipped from the sleeve of his robes as they shifted, but I couldn't tell what it was.


Treebeard spoke reflectively, expressing the change in atmosphere that I, too, could feel with the death of Saruman. “The filth of Saruman is washing away. Trees will come back to live here. Young trees,” he added wistfully, as if envying them their chance to reclaim the evil place for nature. “Wild trees.”


Meanwhile, Pippin had slipped unnoticed from the back of Aragorn's horse and was wading through the water.


“Pippin!” Aragorn called, bringing everyone's attention to the errant hobbit's actions.


Pippin ignored him, instead reaching down to where something gleamed beneath the surface of the water, and lifting up a large, translucent sphere of what looked like glass, with golden light intermittently piercing the shadows within it. It was a Palantir, one of the last seeing stones, something I had heard about in my youth. Pippin held it reverently in his small hands, his gaze transfixed by the play of light.


“Bless my bark!” Treebeard exclaimed.


Gandalf, however, had ridden up behind Pippin.


“Peregrine Took!” he said sharply, causing the young hobbit to start and look round guiltily. The wizard reached out a hand and said firmly, “I'll take that, my lad.” Pippin's gaze was drawn back to his prize, but Gandalf continued, “Quickly, now.”


Pippin handed over the Palantir reluctantly and Gandalf covered it with a fold of his cloak, uttering a grunt of displeasure and directing a sharp glare towards the hobbit. Pippin looked down at his feet, hurt and disappointment clear on his face, his expression turning to resentment as Gandalf turned Shadowfax away. I didn't like to see how quickly an instrument of Saruman's evil had affected little Pippin, and I was glad Gandalf had acted so promptly to remove him from its influence.


* * *


Our purpose at Orthanc thwarted by Wormtongue's rash act, we left Treebeard to watch over the site, and turned our horses towards Edoras. The sense of urgency that had led speed to our journey to Isengard remained with us as we rode; the threat from Saruman was now over, but the over-riding danger of Sauron hung over us yet. I could tell that Theoden and Eomer were eager to reach their home and see that their people were safe, while the set of Gandalf's shoulders showed the tension of Saruman's arrogant words. The wizard would not rest until the whole of Middle Earth was free.


However, people cannot maintain such emotion indefinitely, and the atmosphere as we entered the walls of Edoras was one of celebration. As far as those brave people were concerned, a major battle had been won and their enemy defeated, and they wanted to enjoy the respite from fear and violence that their home afforded them. Theoden gathered all the people who would fit into his great hall but, before the celebration could begin, he charged them not to forget those whose lives had been lost the night before.


“Tonight we remember those who gave their blood to defend this country. Hail the victorious dead!”


The people echoed this shout and everyone raised a glass to honour their missing countrymen. The wine in my own cup tasted bitter, as Theoden's words brought back the memory of Haldir's death, which had been suppressed by my relief and joy at being reunited with the hobbits. I appreciated the Rohan tradition of celebrating the sacrifice of those who fell in battle, but I could not yet look upon the loss of Haldir in that light, despite my earlier words to Gimli.


Once this solemn ceremony was over, musicians struck up their instruments and all the people broke up into smaller groups, to enjoy the company of loved ones who had survived, or to comfort each other in their grief for those who had died. I was at a loss as to what to do with myself, as I had no-one with whom to share my loss, and I did not feel up to being sociable.


Eomer arrived at my side and solved my dilemma with an arm acrosss my shoulders.


“Come, friend,” he said. “You look like you could use a drink. Let me show you the hospitality of the Rohan.”


I let myself be led away to a table where Gimli was already seated beside a barrel.


“Ah, Legolas, lad!” he exclaimed on seeing me. “Try some of this ale with me. I'd wager you'll be lying under the table after one tankard, and that's something I'd like to see!”


At these words, a broad grin spread across Eomer's features. He spread his arms and addressed the crowd. “You have all heard the challenge spoken! You will all witness the outcome, and see whether the elf can prove the dwarf wrong!”


There were hearty cheers and I found a frothing tankard thrust into my hands, while another was presented to Gimli.


Eomer looked at us with mock sternness. “No pauses. No spills,” he admonished.


“And no regurgitation!” Gimli added, much to the amusement of the growing crowd around us.


I looked at the drink I was holding and then up at Eomer as it dawned on me what was happening. “So, it's a drinking game.”


Gimli raised his glass to me and confirmed, “Last one standing wins.”


Someone in the crowd called out, “What shall we drink to?”


Answering shouts reverberated around the room. “To victory! To victory!”


All the men around us took a drink, and Gimli upended his tankard, guzzling down the ale with abandon. I was not sure this drinking game was a very good idea, and I couldn't join in the hearty victory cheer, but I would drink to Haldir and perhaps the Rohan ale would numb the pain that still stabbed at my heart. I took a cautious sniff of the drink and then gulped it down. It was rough, but not unpleasant, and I awaited its affect but nothing happened.


“I don't feel anything,” I said to Eomer in puzzlement, and he laughed heartily.


“The dwarf has already been proven wrong!” he cried. “But the stakes have changed now – you keep drinking until one of you can drink no more. Here – see if a second makes any difference.”


The Rohan warrior next to the barrel kept passing out the drinks, and Gimli and I kept drinking them but, while the taste began to get more pleasant, I still didn't feel any different. Gimli, on the other hand, slowly got less and less comprehensible in the comments he made between drinks, until he finally started making no sense at all.


On about our fifteenth tankard, he spluttered a little, turned to me with an extremely serious expression on his face, and said with some force, “It's the dwarves that go swimming with little, hairy women.”


I decided not to ask him to explain himself, as I was quite certain I didn't want any more details on that statement. I exchanged a glance with Eomer, who was looking at me with undisguised admiration on his face, evidently because I was still standing up with ease, while Gimli was swaying in his chair.


Suddenly, though, a strange sensation started spreading through my left hand, and I brought it up to my face to look at it.


“I feel something,” I said with some surprise. Eomer raised an eyebrow. “A slight tingling in my fingers. I think it's affecting me.”


At that, Gimli spoke up again. “What did I say?” he exclaimed in triumph. “He can't hold his liquor.” At which point, the dwarf went cross-eyed and toppled over backwards onto the floor.


I smiled at Eomer with a feeling of satisfaction that I had defeated Gimli, albeit in a rather ridiculous contest, and Eomer declared, “Game over.”


The Rohirrim surrounding us all cheered and started slapping me on the back, which nearly sent me to the floor as well, but I managed to keep my feet, and took their congratulations with good humour. However, the game had not aided me the way I had hoped, and I still felt unwilling to engage in the hearty camaraderie of these sturdy fold who were my hosts. I excused myself as soon as was polite, and left them to pick up Gimli and deposit him somewhere out of the way. I slipped out of the hall, grabbed a cloak from my pack and found my way outside, away from the noise, where I could examine my thoughts.


I realised it was stupid to seek forgetfulness in the bottom of a tankard. That did no honour to Haldir's memory, and would not achieve any peace in my heart in the long run. What I needed was some solitude, away from revellers and concerned friends alike, where I could think about my friend and come to terms with his loss.


I found a raised platform of paving stones, which looked out over Edoras, and sat down to meditate. I closed my eyes and brought a picture of Haldir to the forefront of my mind. He looked at me reproachfully and I whispered, “I'm sorry.”


Images of the times I had seen him and the occasions we had spent together flitted through my mind. He was a comfort and a companion, but nothing more to me. The ache in my heart had more to do with guilt than selfish pain at separation. I had possessed the means to make him happy and I had kept it from him. However, looking back over the years of our friendship, I saw that even this was a misrepresentation of events, a skewed version that placed far more importance on my place in his life that I warranted. He had many friends and lovers, a vital role in the safeguarding of Lothlorien, many pursuits and skills, none of which had anything to do with me. His love for me was a casual thing that had brightened both our lives when we had been together, but no caused him any undue pain when we were apart..


The picture of Haldir's face shook his head slowly in amusement at my self-importance and smiled at my realisation that his life had been full of so much more than yearning for me. Then, he turned from me, and faded away. With him went the black despair that had been growing deep inside me and, at last, I felt peace. I would miss him and think of him fondly, but no longer would I torture myself over his lost happiness.


I raised myself up off the cold stone and turned my attention to more pressing matters. The red glow hanging over Mordor shifted and writhed in the distance, like an amorphous creature awaiting its moment to strike. The malice emanating from that evil place pressed upon me, its weight attempting to crush my spirit. But I was determined that it would not succeed; if grief for a friend could not defeat me, the machinations of an arrogant would-be god certainly would not, either.


As dawn approached, spreading its promise of light and warmth across the open landscape of Rohan, I heard a step on the paving stones behind me, and knew that Aragorn had come to seek me out. I did not want to speak of what was in my heart, nor discuss anything that had happened to us or between us in the last two days, so I pre-empted him and directed his attention to the ugly view.


“The stars are veiled,” I said. “Something stirs in the east. A sleepless malice.” We exchanged a glance, and I could tell he knew that the melodrama of my words was masking subjects I did not wish to raise. He said nothing, respecting my desire for silence between us, at least for the moment.


Then, the force of Sauron's malice drove towards me in a violent spike, as if it was suddenly drawn away from Mordor.


“The eye of the enemy is moving,” I told Aragorn, as the focus of that attention fixed within the building behind us. “He is here.”


We ran as one, Aragorn presumably heading to where he knew the others were, while I followed the pull of evil as if it were a string attached to my heart, leading me to my destiny. We burst in upon a scene of chaos. Pippin was writhing around on the floor, his hands seemingly clamped to the Palantir, which was glowing brightly. Merry knelt on the other side of the room, his face stricken with fear and horror.


Aragorn acted without thought or consideration for his own safety, grabbing the evil device from the hobbit. His entire body convulsed on contact with it, and he fell to his knees, his teeth gritted against its influence. I rushed to help him, clasping his shoulders and passing what energy I had to fight it into him. Whether by his own will, or through my aid, Aragron managed to release the ball, slumping against my chest, obviously thoroughly shaken by even such short contact with Sauron's mind.


Roused by the commotion, Gandalf chased the rolling Palantir across the floor of the chamber and threw a blanket over it. As if a lamp had been extinguished, the awful pressure of our enemy's presence amongst us was cut off, and I felt a darkness lift from my mind. Aragorn had slipped further to the floor, but now looked up into my concerned eyes with a reassuring smile. I helped him sit up, and he did not move away from my hands, his body still trembling beneath my fingers.


Merry rushed to his friend's side, crying, “Pippin!” and bringing all our attention back to the smallest of our party, who lay, unmoving, on the stone floor, his eyes open and his face slack.


Gandalf cursed, “Fool of a Took!” echoing his words in the depths of Moria after Pippin had knocked the orc skeleton down the well, bringing the hordes down upon us in the mine. This time, though, his voice held more fear than anger, and I found myself fervently hoping the foolish Took would survive this latest misadventure to bring disaster upon us once again.


The wizard hurried to where the hobbits were, pushing poor Merry out of his way as he knelt down and grabbed Pippin's small hand. He laid his own over Pippin's face and muttered something I could not make out. His hand moved across to rest on Pippin's cheek and, suddenly, Pippin came awake, gasping as if he had run up ten flights of stairs.


“Look at me,” the wizard commanded.


Pippin replied, “Gandalf, forgive me.” His voice was so small and desperate that I knew the punishment he had already received far outweighed the crime of stealing the Palantir. He closed his eyes and tried to turn his face away, but Gandalf pulled his gaze back.


“Look at me,” he commanded again. “What did you see?”


“A tree,” Pippin whispered, his eyes now wide and fearful. “There was a white tree, in a courtyard of stone. It was dead. The city was burning.”


I felt Aragorn tense, and Gandalf soon explained why.


“Minas Tirith,” he interpreted. “Is that what you saw?”


Pippin continued, the fear in his voice growing audibly with every word. “I saw – I saw him. I could hear his voice in my head.”


“And what did you tell him?” Gandalf's words were harsh. “Speak!”


“He asked me my name. I didn't answer. He hurt me.” Pippin's face screwed up with remembered pain, but he kept his eyes fixed on Gandalf's.


“What did you tell him about Frodo and the Ring?” the wizard demanded.


“Nothing!” Pippin protested. “Nothing, Gandalf. I promise!” Tears leaked out of the corners of his eyes and trickled slowly down his pale face. “I'm sure I would have told him whatever he wanted to know, though, if Aragorn hadn't taken the stone from me. I'm sorry! I'm so sorry!”


Gandalf rose to his feet, ignoring the weeping hobbit, and turning his stern visage to Aragorn. “We must raise King Theoden. We now know where Sauron will strike. We must decide how to use this knowledge to our advantage.”


He crossed to where his robes lay and put them on hurriedly, then swept from the room. Aragorn and I spared a glance for little Pippin, who still lay on the floor with Merry's arms now around him, then followed Gandalf out into the castle.


* * * * *


By the time we had managed to find a servant to fetch the king, and had gathered everyone in the hall of Meduseld, the morning sunlight was streaming in through the high windows. The difference in atmosphere from the revelries of the night before was palpable. My mind wandered slightly as Gandalf recounted what had happened, and I found myself hoping that last night would not be the last celebration to echo around these walls. Gandalf's next pronouncement brought me back to the present proceedings.


“There was no lie in Pippin's eyes. A fool, but an honest fool he remains. He told Sauron nothing of Frodo and the Ring.”


This elicited an audible sigh of relief from Gimli, though I noticed Pippin dropping his eyes to the floor shamefacedly on the other side of the room. His cousin stood staunchly by his side, though his face betrayed fear and anger in equal measure at his young relative's actions.


“We've been strangely fortunate,” Gandalf continued. “Pippin saw in the Palantir a glimpse of the enemy's plan. Sauron moves to strike the city of Minas Tirith. His defeat at Helm's Deep showed our enemy one thing. He knows the heir of Elendil has come forth.”


He paused to throw a significant glance at Aragorn, who stood straighter under his gaze. I felt a shiver of foreboding that I did not want explained.


“There is courage still, strength enough perhaps to challenge him,” Gandalf went on. “Sauron fears this. He will not risk the peoples of Middle Earth uniting under one banner. He will raze Minas Tirith to the ground before he sees a king return to the throne of men.”


I was still watching Aragorn and I saw the look of apprehension that crossed his face at these words. I knew he would not try to evade his duty, though I also knew the throne of Gondor held no appeal for him.


Gandalf still continued in his speech. “If the beacons of Minas Tirith are lit, Rohan must be ready for war.”


However, it seemed his words had had little effect on our current host.


“Tell me,” Theoden asked, “why should we ride to the aid of those who did not come to ours?”


We all stared at him in disbelief, unable to comprehend his attitude.


“What do we owe Gondor?” he demanded.


Aragorn ignored him, stating instead, “I will go.”


“No.” Gandalf was firm.


“They must be warned,” Aragorn persisted.


“They will be,” Gandalf reassured him, then moved close to speak more quietly to Aragorn. “You must come to Minas Tirith by another road. Follow the river. Look to the black ships.”


I did not know what he meant, but the shiver of foreboding skittered down my spine again at his words. I vowed silently to myself and I would not let Aragorn out of my sight along whatever path he might take.


“I ride for Minas Tirith,” Gandalf stated firmly. “And I won't be going alone.” He looked across at Pippin. “You, my lad, will be coming with me.” He gave Pippin no time to react. “You've already got your cloak on – good. Come along quickly, now. We've a long way to go, and the daylight won't wait for us.” With that, he strode from the hall, obviously expecting Pippin to follow in his wake.


Pippin looked at Merry, who shrugged his shoulders. “Don't look at me,” he said, irritably. “This is one mess I can't get you out of. Come on, we'd better go.” Merry took Pippin's arm and practically dragged him from his seat, leading him after the wizard.


And that was the last I saw of Pippin for quite some time. After such a happy reunion, our group had been sundered once more. As I thought of Pippin being separated from the last of his friends, my mind also turned to Sam and Frodo, who were somewhere out in the wilderness, really alone and with so much still resting on them. I hoped they were faring well.


* * * * *


Gimli, Aragorn, Merry and I settled into an uneasy existence at Edoras, while we waited for some sign from our absent friends. Gimli said to me on the afternoon of the day they left that we should make the most of this interval of peace to rest and regain our strength. He was right, of course, but it wasn't that easy. The threat of Mordor hung over us all still, like a huge boulder on a slowly fraying string above our heads.


More than that, Aragorn was restless, uncomfortable in the knowledge that Theoden was unwilling to bring his people further into the war. I discussed the situation with him on more than one occasion and found him torn between a desire to argue the point with the Rohan king and a fear of highlighting the distinction between their senses of duty when he was housed as a guest in Theoden's court.


Merry, on the other hand, had no interest in the wider aspect of events. All his thoughts were bent on Pippin and what might be happening to him now that he was out of his cousin's company.


I came upon him one morning, standing on the platform outside the Hall of Meduseld, his eyes fixed on the horizon in the direction of Minas Tirith. I approached him and laid a friendly hand on his shoulder.


“Pippin will be alright, Merry,” I reassured him. “He has Gandalf to protect him, after all.”


“How can you be so sure?” the young hobbit replied, doubt clouding his words. “You've seen how much trouble Pippin can get himself into, and that was when I was around to watch out for him. I didn't have anything to do but look after him, and he still orchestrated several disasters. Gandalf will have many much more important things to take his attention away from Pippin. And in a huge city, full of strangers, just think what kind of mischief he could end up in!”


He had a point. “That may be true,” I conceded. “However, it may be that his experience with the Palantir has taught him a valuable lesson. He seemed very shaken by it, as I can well imagine I would be, and that may temper his behaviour from now on. Perhaps his separation from you will also have an effect – without you to get him out of trouble, he may think more before he gets himself into it. It may be that now is the time for young Peregrine Took to finally grow up a little.”


“But he's so far away,” Merry said, his voice trembling a little. “And he's gone into a war zone. That's no place for a hobbit. Even if he doesn't bring trouble down on himself, how will he be able to avoid it, when Sauron attacks the city?” He looked up at me solemnly. “And what if I don't want him to grow up? He may be stupid and foolish and annoying, but he wouldn't be Pippin if he wasn't. What if when we meet again, he's changed and he doesn't need me any more?”


These were questions I could not answer, so I simply squeezed Merry's shoulder and stood silently with him while he worried.


* * * * *


On the morning of the fifth day after Pippin and Gandalf's departure, I was sitting with Gimli inside the Hall of Meduseld, finishing off breakfast. We had been discussing Aragorn's restlessness and propensity for spending most of his time watching the Edoras end of the beacon line. Suddenly, the doors burst open and Aragorn rushed in, skidding to a halt just inside the threshold. Everyone stared at him in alarm.


“The beacons of Minas Tirith! The beacons are lit! Gondor calls for aid!”


I could see the desperate hope in his face that Theoden would change his mind about helping Gondor and, beneath that, the rigid determination to act even without Theoden.


The hall remained silent while everyone turned their attention to Theoden expectantly.


After several long moments, he rose to the challenge of the moment, declaring proudly, “And Rohan will answer! Muster the Rohirrim!”


Eomer exchanged a long look with his sister and touched her shoulder with affection before bowing and leaving the hall to obey his king's command.


Aragorn stepped up before Theoden, eyeing him as an equal but offering his services as a subordinate. “Give me and my companions whatever orders are necessary for us to aid in your preparations, my lord. We are at your disposal for as long as fate keeps us in your company.”


“Well, then,” Theoden replied gravely. “Make ready your horses and equipment. We will ride within the hour.”


It took me far less time than that to gather my belongings and saddle Arod, so I was already waiting in the courtyard when the king emerged from the hall to give instructions to his men.


“Assemble the army at Dunharrow; as many men as can be found. You have two days,” he told Eomer, who set off immediately. “Gamling, make haste across the Riddermark. Summon every able-bodied man to Dunharrow.”


Edoras was a hive of organised chaos, with men and horses moving everywhere, but everyone seeming to know exactly where they should be and never getting in anyone else's way. Gimli joined me in a corner, where I was attempting to prevent my presence causing any disruption.


“I saw yon wench, Eowyn, readying her horse,” he told me, a glint in his eye. “I swear that girl would join us on the battlefield if she could find a way. She made sure she was close to Aragorn in the procession as well. She's not going to give up on him easily.”


“Then he should make his feelings more clear to her,” I replied softly. “It does no good for him to be charming and polite. It'll only make her think she has a chance with him and make things harder for the both of them in the long run.”


Gimli looked at me appraisingly. “Clear signals haven't helped you any, though, have they?” He cast his eyes to the sky. “What is it about that boy, that has everyone within range drooling over him? I mean, I love the lad like a brother, but I just can't see it myself.”


I grinned at him. “Come on, old dwarf It's time to get you back in the saddle.”


He grumbled but complied. As we rode Arod towards the gates, he snorted contemptuously. “Horsemen! I wish I could muster a legion of Dwarves, fully armed and filthy.”


“Your kinsmen have no need to ride to war,” I told him, expressing the dark shadow in my heart. “I fear war already marches on their lands.”


He grunted uncertainly, and we rode the rest of the way in silence.


Eomer had gathered his soldiers at the gate. “Now is the hour, Riders of Rohan!” he cried. “Oaths you have taken – now fulfil them all! To Lord and Land!”


There was a great cheer, and the Rohirrim set out to war.


* * * * *


Eomer and Gamling must have ridden like the wind to wherever it was they went since, when we rode into Dunharrow the following day, there were a significant number of warriors there before us. The gathering did not seem to impress Theoden, however.


“How many?” he called out to a soldier standing at the side of the roadway.


“I bring five hundred men from the Westfold, my lord,” came the reply.


“We have three hundred more from Fenmarch,” Gamling reported.


Theoden asked, “Where are the riders from Snowbourn?” His face was grim, and I could tell that he was not happy with the turnout. Gamling's next words did not help matters.


“None have come, my lord.”


Theoden led us, and all those who had come with us from Edoras, up a steep and winding path to the top of a cliff, overlooking the encampment. It was not long before tents had sprouted up all over the grassy field, one being assigned to Gimli and myself, with Arod tethered nearby. Aragorn stayed with Theoden, so Gimli and I explored the camp by ourselves.


There was a crack in the mountain at the far side of the clearing, and I noticed several men having difficulty controlling their horses near it. The animals whickered and pranced, unwilling to stay long in the vicinity of the pathway. I thought I knew the reason why and, spotting Eomer, I walked over to him, Gimli following at my back.


“The horses are restless,” I said by way of broaching the subject. “And the men are quiet.”


“They grow nervous in the shadow of the mountain,” Eomer replied, gesturing at the track behind us.


Gimli was more direct, as always. “That road there; where does that lead?”


I hazarded my guess. “It is the road to the Dimholt; the door under the mountain.”


“None who venture there ever return. That mountain is evil,” Eomer confirmed.


As we spoke, Aragorn left Theoden's side and wandered towards the opening of the pathway, an intent look on his face. He stood for a moment, then started as if he had seen something in the shadows.


Oblivious, Gimli stomped up beside him and laid a hand on his arm. Ever sure to have his priorities straight, he said, “Aragorn. Let's find some food.”


Aragorn allowed himself to be led away, but he looked back towards the mountain as he moved. I raised an eyebrow of enquiry at him, but he just shrugged and shook his head. He looked a little shaken, though, and I wondered what he thought he had seen.


A kitchen had been set up a little way away from our accommodations, so we collected some stew and found a place out of the way to enjoy our lunch.


Aragorn was serious. “King Theoden does not believe many more men will be coming to join us. I am worried that our efforts will be in vain. We have already seen the number of troops Saruman commanded. Surely the army of Sauron himself will be far greater. Those we have here will not be able to match them on an open battlefield.”


“But we must try,” I reminded him needlessly. “Better to die in battle than be slaughtered in our homes as we await our doom without lifting a finger.”


“Of course, Legolas,” he replied. “So there is little point in worrying about it now, is there? We will do what we can, and hope for the best.”


“And in the meantime, we'll enjoy our stew!” Gimli said, ever the pragmatist.


Aragorn and I could not help smiling at him, and I wondered at how my opinion of the gruff dwarf had changed since that first meeting at Rivendell, that seemed so long ago.


* * * * *


Later, I lay awake in my tent, listening to Gimli snore, and thinking about what Aragorn had said. Where was our hope now? Was the only thing we had to look forward to really death at the hands of Saruman's army, on the fields before Minas Tirith? Surely, after all our efforts and sacrifices, our journey would not end like that.


As if in answer to my thoughts, I felt a familiar presence nearby. I recognised it as Elrond, but why was he here? With what little mental power I had at my command, I sent a questing thought towards him. In return, I received an image of Aragorn, a mighty sword, and the dark mountain that rose above us.


The shards of Narsil had been re-forged!


Immediately, I roused Gimli. He came awake with a grunt and glared at me ferociously.


“What is it?”


“Elrond is here,” I told him quickly. “The blade that was broken has been re-forged. He has come to push Aragorn towards his destiny. And we must be there to accompany him.”


“Why must you always speak in riddles?” Gimli grumbled. “Damned elves and their pretentious ways.”


I sighed in frustration. “Aragorn is about to leave the camp, to travel the Dimholt road.”


“Well, why didn't you just say so? I'll get my axe.”


We dressed and collected our gear as quickly as possible, and I saddled Arod while Gimli huffed impatiently. Within a few minutes, we were making our way through the dark of the camp towards Aragorn's tent. There were still men about and they watched us with interest. As we approached, I heard voices and pulled Arod to a halt, gesturing for Gimli to be quiet. Eowyn's words drifted out of the darkness towards us.


“Why are you doing this? The war lies to the East, you cannot leave on the eve of battle!” There was a brief pause before she continued. “You cannot abandon the men.” I knew that was not what she really wanted to say, and my heart went out to her in understanding that only I could truly give.


“Eowyn...” Aragorn sounded tired.


“We need you here,” she persisted, pleadingly.


“Why have you come?” came his reply, cutting to the heart of the matter and urging her to speak more plainly.


She was silent for a long moment, then she said softly, “Do you not know?”


I felt Aragorn's sorrow as he sighed, and I imagined that he spoke his next words to me as well as to the lovely young woman who stood before him with her heart in her eyes.


“It is but a shadow and a thought that you love. I have wished you joy since first I saw you.” He spoke gently and with great affection as he took away her hope. “I cannot give you what you seek.”


I exchanged a pointed glance with Gimli, who looked uncomfortable at having overheard such a private moment.


As Aragorn came into view, leading Brego towards the mountain, Gimli stomped up behind him and demanded, “Just where do you think you're off to?”


Aragorn set his features into what he must have thought was fierce determination. “Not this time. This time you must stay, Gimli.”


But the dwarf resolutely stood his ground, and I came up to join him.


“Have you heard nothing of the stubbornness of dwarves?” I said lightly, trying to relieve the tension of the moment.


“Might as well accept it,” Gimli reasoned. “We're going with you, laddie.”


Yes, I thought, I have made a promise and I do not intend to break it now, not even if it means travelling beneath the mountain and perhaps never coming out.


Aragorn smiled, and led us down the Dimholt road, with the alarmed shouts and angry questions of the Rohirrim to send us on our way and, somewhere, a golden-haired maiden watching us sadly as we left.


* * * * *


We rode in silence as the dawn brought light to the landscape around us. I could sense that the horses were nervous; Arod's muscles were clenched beneath me and his ears twitched at every tiny sound of our progress. I tried to convey calm through my body into his, but I fear my own apprehension prevented me from being very convincing.


The rocks around us provided a path of unending greay, surrounded by hostile walls and treacherous footholds.


At last, Gimli broke the silence. “What kind of army would linger in such a place?”

His voice brought my mind back to my companions, and I was glad of an excuse to throw words into the dead air that felt steeped with oppression.


“One that is cursed,” I told him, dredging up the story from the depths of my memory. “Long ago, the Men of the Mountain swore an oath to the last king of Gondor. To come to his aid, to fight. But when the time came, when Gondor's need was dire, they fled – vanishing into the darkness of the mountain.” I suppressed a shudder at the thought of them trying to save themselves by fleeing into this cold, empty place. “And so Isildur cursed them; never to rest, until they had fulfilled their pledge.” I lost myself for a moment in the old tale, reciting the prophecy that concluded it. “Who shall call them from the grey twilight? The forgotten people. The heir of him to whom the oath they swore. From the north shall he come. Need shall drive him. He shall pass the door to the Paths of the Dead.”


And this is where we found ourselves. As the echo of those last words faded away, the door came into view before us, a dark hole cut into the side of the mountain, leading into a pit, the contents of which no man had ever returned to tell. We dismounted from the horses and led them forwards slowly. A breath of air wafted out of the opening, bringing with it a sour feeling of decay that set the hairs on the back of my neck on edge.


“The very warmth of my blood seems drawn away,” Gimli muttered, betraying a greater sensitivity than I had credited him with.


The words above the opening drew my eye and I read them aloud. “The way is shut. It was made by those who are dead. And the dead keep it. The way is shut.”


As I spoke, a sudden blast of wind erupted from the doorway, the air mingled with faint cries of anguish and torment. Both horses started up and pulled away from us, running in fear back the way they had come.


“Brego!” Aragorn called, but did not move to follow.


Instead, he turned resolutely to the entrance, cried, “I do not fear death!” and strode into the blackness.


I exchanged a fearful glance with Gimli, but there was no choice in the matter for me. Taking a deep breath, I steeled myself for whatever terrors lay ahead and followed Aragorn, as I had pledged I would.


I heard Gimli behind me gearing himself up. “Well, this is something unheard of! An Elf will go underground where a Dwarf dare not? Ahh, I'll never hear the end of it!”


His footsteps sounded in the darkness behind me, and I was glad of his solid presence at my back.


I soon caught up with Aragorn, who had slowed to a cautious walk as the tunnel opened out ahead of us. Light emanated from a strange mist that circled us, clinging to our clothes as if trying to detain us. I peered ahead and gasped as I saw the mist coalescing into recognisable forms.


“What is it?” called Gimli from behind me, obviously alarmed by my reaction. “What do you see?”


“I see shapes of men,” I told him. “And of horses.”


“Where?” he demanded, clearly unable to see them for himself.


The mist swirled in and out of my awareness, mesmerising me with its movements, forming clear shapes and then dissolving away in a maddening fashion that kept my gaze searching for solidity within it.


“Pale banners like shreds of cloud,” I murmured, describing what I saw as it changed before my eyes. “Spears rising... like winter thickets through a shroud of mist.” I caught a sense of foreboding in the mist and suddenly knew from whence it emanated. “The Dead are following. They have been summoned.” Summoned by our presence, I surmised. After all, they claimed to guard the passage, not allowing anything that lived to see the other side.


Gimli harrumphed. “The Dead? Summoned? I knew that.” He tried to hide his nervousness with a chuckle. “Very good. Very good.”


His mutterings grew fainter as I followed Aragorn further into the cavern. Then, a shout of, “Legolas!” told me the dwarf was once again hurrying to keep up with us.


As we progressed, the mist surrounding us started to form into hands, with fingers that grasped at us. I lifted my arms away from the ghostly fingers and pressed on. Soon afterwards, I stepped on something that crunched beneath my feet. Aragorn warned us not to look down at the ground, but it was impossible to resist the temptation. What greeted my peering gaze were piles of human skulls, packed into the ground in such a way that it was impossible to avoid treading on them. Nonetheless, I ventured onwards, careful to keep Aragorn in sight, and also making sure that Gimli did not get too far behind.


Eventually, the tunnel broadened out even more into a large open space. We stopped before a set of huge double doors, which dominated one wall of the cavern. An eerie feeling filled the air around us and I could sense countless unseen eyes viewing us from some hidden recess. Whoever dwelt here was well aware that we were there; all we could do was wait for them to show themselves.


It did not take long. As we took in our surroundings, a disembodied voice suddenly called out, “Who enters my domain?”


I spun back round to face the doors, and a ghost-like figure appeared on the steps in front of them. It carried a sword and wore a crown, its long hair framing a face that was part flesh and part exposed bone. It seemed to be made out of the mist we had seen before, its figure exuding a sickly green light.


Aragorn stepped forward calmly and replied, “One who would have your allegiance.”


“The dead do not suffer the living to pass.”


I could feel Gimli's tension as he stood beside me, his gaze riveted to the apparition. Aragaorn, however, remained utterly unfazed.


He said firmly, “You will suffer me!”


The ghost king let out a laugh of pure contempt. As if this was a signal to advance, more shapes appeared out of the darkness, moving forwards to completely surround us. They were all formed from the green mist, all dressed as soldiers, but not whole men. Some had skeletal arms, others sported missing features; all were clearly hostile.


Their king pronounced, “The way is shut. It was made by those who are dead, and the dead keep it.” His soldiers pressed closer, and he repeated, “The way is shut. Now you must die.”


He moved towards Aragorn, and my instincts took over. I know it was stupid to think loosing an arrow at a ghost would be effective, but I was working on pure impulse, attempting to protect my friend. Unsurprisingly, the arrow passed straight through the ghost's head.


Both ghost and Aragorn ignored my attempted intervention.


Aragorn stated, “I summon you to fulfil your oath.”


“None but the king of Gondor may command me!” the ghost claimed.


Then, it swung its sword at Aragorn, who blocked the blow expertly with his newly forged blade. The metal clashed together, clearly startling the ghost king, who found himself with the point of an elven sword against his neck.


“That line was broken!” he cried in disbelief.


I had never heard Aragorn sound so confident and determined as when he replied, “It has been remade.” This was a man facing up to his destiny, prepared to do whatever it might take to get the job done.


Aragorn pushed the ghost king forcibly back, and all the misty figures stood and watched the confrontation in silence. The atmosphere was tense. I realised that, if the king commanded the soldiers to attack us, Aragorn would be only one who could stand against them, and even he could not face so many without defeat.


“Fight for us and regain your honour,” he offered, speaking now to the whole host. “What say you?”


He began to walk a circle around myself and Gimli, staying between us and the ghostly soldiers, holding out his sword for them to see.


“What say you?” he repeated more urgently.


Gimli said, “You waste your time, Aragorn! They had no honour in life, they have none now in death.”


Aragorn ignored him, focusing all his attention on the troops lined up before him. “I am Isildur's heir. Fight for me! And I will hold your oaths fulfilled!” He changed direction and circled the king, repeating, “What say you?”


I could see in him a great military leader, who would be able to command the love and loyalty of any men who served under him. But these were not men, they were shadows of former traitors, who would not be swayed by impassioned rhetoric. The king merely laughed and all the soldiers began to melt away.


Desperate now, Aragorn cried, “You have my word! Fight, and I will release you from this living death! What say you?”


They gave their answer by vanishing as quickly as they had appeared, the king's laughter echoing around us as we stood in the darkness.


Gimli shouted, “Stand, you traitors!” but they were gone.


A chill wind sprung up from nowhere, and a distant cracking sound alerted me to coming danger. Before I could act, however, hundreds of skulls started pouring out of the crumbling walls beside the gates. In seconds, we were deluged with them, an avalanche of skulls descending upon us, until we were knee deep in them.


“Legolas! Run!” Aragorn called, spurring me into action.


I grabbed Gimli and shoved him ahead of me as the skulls continued to rain down, seemingly without end. We fought our way through them, struggling to maintain our footing, choking on the dust brought down with them. I thought for a moment we would die there, buried beneath the remains of those from whom we had come to seek help.


However, at last, daylight shone ahead and we burst out on the other side of the mountain. The sudden brightness after such gloom dazzled my eyes but, when my vision cleared, I looked down to see the river below us. On the water, the black-sailed ships of Sauron's mercenaries hove into view, on their way to Minas Tirith.


Aragorn took a couple of steps and then dropped to his knees in the grass, despair pouring from him in waves. Knowing that words would do no good, I moved up to him and put my arm around his shoulders, offering my mute support. In a rare moment of weakness, he leaned back against me, and I felt his sorrow at his failure. I took no pleasure in the feel of his body resting against mine, only grateful that he felt able to accept comfort from me.


We remained there a long moment, as the ships sailed towards us, unstoppable in their numbers, with only us three ranged against them. Then, the sound of rushing wind came from behind us. Aragorn rose and turned back towards the mountain, Gimli and I mirroring his movements. As we watched, the ghost king melted through the rock to stand before us in the open air.


He said simply, “We fight.”


Aragorn closed his eyes briefly, a smile of relief brushing his lips. Then he nodded acknowledgement to the ghost king and said simply, “Thank you.”


“We do not fight for your thanks,” the apparition replied flatly. “We fight for our freedom. We weary of our endless existence here; we desire release.”


“And that is what I shall grant you once our battle is done,” Aragorn promised. “First we must deal with the ships below. Wait for my signal before you join the fray.”


The ghost king nodded and melted from view.


Aragorn turned back towards the river, his demeanour determined. It warmed my heart to see his confidence renewed. With a glance at us, he strode down the hillside, not needing to tell us to follow him. Gimli grinned and gestured for me to precede him.


When the three of us reached the river bank, the first ship was just approaching, enabling us to converse easily with the crew, a motley-looking bunch of mercenaries.


Aragorn stood firm on the bank, his body radiating charisma, as if he could halt all the ships with just a glance.


“You may go no further,” he called out. “You will not enter Gondor.”


One of the crew stepped forward, presumably the captain, though there was nothing in his apparel to distinguish him from the others.


“Who are you to deny us passage?” he demanded contemptuously.


Aragorn spoke casually over his shoulder. “Legolas, fire a warning shot past the bosun's ear.”


Smiling, I drew an arrow from my quiver and set it to my bow. As I pulled back the string to fire, Gimli warned me to watch my aim and bumped my arm just as I let the arrow fly. It struck the bosun full in the chest and he toppled to the deck. Outraged as his interference, I threw Gimli an angry glance, but he seemed unrepentant.


“That's it,” he called. “Right. We warned you. Prepare to be boarded.”


Little affected by the death of their comrade, the corsairs laughed uproariously at the dwarf's seemingly ridiculous statement.


The captain said, “Boarded? By you and whose army?”


This, of course, was the moment Aragorn had been waiting for.


“This army,” he announced with a grim smile, giving the signal to the ghost king to appear.


In a dramatic gesture I had not expected from the dead ruler, the apparition swept through Aragorn as he appeared, making it look as if Aragorn had summoned him from within himself. The ranks of soldiers formed up behind their leader and swept across the distance between the bank and the ship. Their insubstantiality allowed them to float over the water, and they swarmed over the vessels in their hundreds. The amusement of the corsairs rapidly turned to terror as they were attacked by enemies they quickly discovered they could not harm. The battle was a short one.


When it was over, I felt somewhat dissatisfied. The three of us had not contributed at all, being stuck on the bank, but we had caused the death of all those men, giving them no chance to defend themselves. It seemed wrong.


Gimli obviously picked up on my mood, coming to stand next to me. “We gave them the opportunity to surrender,” he pointed out weakly. I looked askance at him. “Yes, well,” he continued. “You've got to look at the bigger picture, Legolas. It may not be the best way to wage a war, but we've got to use the tools at our disposal. Surely, you'd not rather let Sauron win?”


“Of course not,” I replied. “I know you're right. It's just...” I gestured expansively at the fleet of ships on which so many live had just been extinguished.


Gimli patted me on the arm. “I know, lad, I know.”


The ghost army took up posts on the ships, those on the first one throwing us lines so that we could moor it long enough to board ourselves. Then we set sail for Minas Tirith, the first stage of our journey complete.


It was an eerie way to travel, sharing a boat with a ghostly crew, who floated around the deck, completing their tasks without a sound. The fleet of ships followed behind us, as silent as ours. Gimli exchanged many glances with me along the way, grimacing or rolling his eyes at our ethereal companions, though obviously unwilling to make comments about his feelings when in such close proximity and without an easy escape. Aragorn stayed at the helm of the ship, staring ahead with an intensity that did not invite conversation, so it was a less than companionable journey, all told.


The ghost soldiers were evidently competent sailors and clearly needed no assistance from me, so I kept out of the way, stationing myself at the bow and training my gaze on the river ahead.


By the time we reached the docks at Minas Tirith, I was itching for some activity, all my senses on edge and ready for battle. This turned out to be a good thing, since we were hailed almost immediately by orcs on the shore.


“Late as usual! Pirate scum!” one called, obviously mistaking us for the mercenaries hired by Sauron. “There’s knife work here that needs doing! Come on, you sea-rats. Get off your ships!”


As ready as I was, Aragorn leapt over the side of the ship onto the dockside, sword in hand. Gimli and I followed directly behind him, also with our weapons at the ready. Shock registered on the faces of the orcs before us, quickly followed by disdain as no others appeared to support us.


“There are plenty for the both of us,” Gimli told me, referencing the competition at the battle of Helm’s Deep. “May the best dwarf win.”


Aragorn charged into the fray, and the ghost army swarmed off the ships behind us, sweeping across the water from multiple ships to join the battle. I held back, utilising my bow for as long as possible before moving to hand-to-hand fighting. I realised from my experience under the mountain that my weapons could not harm the ghosts, so I did not bother to take note of where they were, firing arrows through them to pierce the orcs on the other side. The arrows did not appear to affect their concentration, so I carried on, hoping I would not have angry ghost soldiers to contend with after the battle.


We fought our way quickly across the docks and out onto the plain in front of the city. I was stopped in my tracks by the sight that greeted us. Thousands upon thousands of men, orcs, trolls and other creatures waged war beneath the walls of Minas Tirith. We had arrived too late to see the start of the battle and it was certainly in full swing now. I only hoped that those friends I knew were on the field were still alright, but had no hope of finding any familiar faces amongst the hordes of fighters. There was no time to think about the greater picture; we were needed and we fought.


I managed to stay close to Gimli as we made our way into the fray, wanting to keep a familiar figure nearby. We bolstered each other by periodically shouting out the count of our kills, which rapidly reached double figures. As Gimli had said at the dock, there were more than enough enemies to keep both of us busy.


Every orc I cut down was instantly replaced by two more. I closed my mind to everything but the battle immediately before me, just keeping a sliver of awareness available to track Gimli’s and Aragorn’s progress across the field.


I heard Aragorn call my name and spun round to see a giant Mumakil bearing down on me. I tracked its movement for a few long moments, then leaped in the air and caught hold of one huge tusk as the creature passed by. From there, I jumped to its front leg, where I could use the many arrows protruding from its tough skin to climb higher. I scrambled up onto its back, firing at the warriors atop it as I went.


“Thirty-three, thirty-four,” I counted almost without thinking.


Several painted soldiers launched themselves at me, but I managed to push them off balance and send them tumbling to the ground below. I heard their screams as they were trampled beneath the Mumkil’s enormous feet, but had no time to dwell on their fate. I grabbed a nearby rope and swung round to the creature’s back legs, noting where the harness joined together to hold the main tower in place. I sliced at the ropes and allowed the weight of the tower sliding of the Mumakil’s back to pull me upright, trying to let go at just the right moment to maintain my balance. I mistimed it slightly and the rope was yanked out of my hand by the falling tower, wrenching my arm painfully. The tower structure crashed to the ground on the far side of the creature, and was instantly swarming with ghost soldiers, who looked like some kind of insect infestation from above.


Now that the Mumakil was free from any other passengers, I strode up to its neck and fired three arrows simultaneously into its brain. It staggered and fell. Aiming for a dramatic finish, I leaped onto its trunk and slid down it, jumping off the end to land with a flourish directly in front of Gimli, who was staring open-mouthed at the massive carcass behind me. I couldn’t resist a self-satisfied grin in his direction, but he feigned being entirely unimpressed.


“That still only counts as one!” he said. “Alright then, come on!” And we turned together to face the enemies that kept on coming.


I don’t know how long we fought. My wrenched arm forced me to resort to hand-to-hand combat, using only one blade, which curtailed my success somewhat. At one point, as I was fighting off one orc, another came at me from behind, grabbing my injured arm and wrenching it further. I felt something give in my shoulder joint and cried out at the pain but managed to keep fighting and kill both orcs. My left arm was completely useless after that and I was sure the shoulder was dislocated at the very least.


Minutes, hours, days later – I could not tell – an orc fell from my sword blade and no other rose up before me to replace it. I staggered slightly as my fighting movements came to a halt, my limbs seemingly moving of their own volition, with no conscious instruction from me. I glanced around me, disoriented for a moment, then my gaze alighted on Aragorn and my heart leapt in relief. Gimli stumped over to my side and grinned.


“One hundred and thirty-eight!” he declared triumphantly. “Beat that!”


It took me a moment to understand what he meant, and he obviously noticed my confusion, as his expression grew concerned.


“Are you hurt, Legolas?” he asked me.


I managed to smile down at him, recovering my composure. “I'm afraid I have to confess to having lost count somewhere along the way. To my mind, that must mean my score was far in excess of one hundred and thirty-eight, as it would take a vast number to cause me to lose track.”


“Gah!” Gimli was unconvinced. “No score means disqualification! I claim the victory!”


I hadn't the energy to argue with him, so I merely turned and led him over to where Aragorn stood before the army of the dead.


“Release us,” the ghost king was saying as we approached.


“Bad idea,” Gimli advised. “Very handy in a tight spot, these lads, despite the fact they're dead.”


I could see his point; the dead army had swung the battle in our favour, defeating the opposing forces with a speed and ruthlessness we would never have achieved without them. However, I knew Aragorn would never betray their trust.


“You gave us your word,” the dead king growled, an unnecessary reminder.


Aragorn declared, “I hold your oath fulfilled. Go. Be at peace.”


The ghostly king smiled with obvious pleasure and slowly melted away, all the soldiers disappearing with him. As Gimli clearly agreed, I thought it would have been useful for them to remain a while longer, as I knew the war was not over yet, but they had done as they had promised and had earned their freedom. I wished them joy of their long-awaited rest.


Gandalf had appeared some time during this exchange, a very welcome sight. He bowed to Aragorn, acknowledging the signs that Aragorn had once more taken up his destiny. There was no opportunity for the wizard to speak, however, since the air was suddenly rent by a cry of pure despair.


We all turned in the direction of the sound to see Eomer flinging himself to the ground some distance away and pulling a body up into his arms. Long, flowing blonde hair spilled over the trampled grass and I realised with a jolt that it was Eowyn who lay unmoving in her brother's embrace. Eomer's continued cries pierced my heart and I sorrowed that such a vibrant flame should be extinguished, even as I wondered how she came to be there on the battlefield.


Aragorn strode to Eomer's side and knelt down next to him, though he seemed oblivious to the presence of anyone else in his overwhelming grief.


Aragorn looked up from Eowyn's body and said, “She yet lives.”


Eomer raised his gaze and stared at him.


“Quickly!” Aragorn commanded. “Bring her inside the city. I may be able to save her.”


Eomer scrambled to his feet, clutching Eowyn to him, and followed Aragorn silently as he strode back across the field towards the city. I could see others roaming the battlefield all around us, scanning the ground for friends, loved ones, anyone who might still be alive. Gimli and I joined the procession heading to the city. I, for one, had no desire to stay among the dead any longer than necessary, and I also wanted to make sure that Aragorn got some rest, as I knew he would drive himself into the ground trying to help the victims of the battle, despite his own exertions.


In fact, I got little chance to see to Aragorn's needs as it was impossible to get near him once he entered the Houses of Healing inside Minas Tirith. There were so many casualties and so few healers, I would not have been able to drag him away if I had tried. I knew he would work until either there were no more patients to tend or he actually collapsed from exhaustion, so I decided to stay and make sure the latter didn't happen. I found a seat on a bench at the back of the main hall and sat down to wait.


I watched him move among the wounded, giving comfort and ministration wherever it was needed, and I marvelled at his energy and dedication. I could see he truly felt responsible for all these people, even though he knew almost none of them. He started, of course, with Eowyn, while Eomer sat nearby, his face a picture of misery and fear. I knew exactly how he felt; helpless and utterly frustrated by the feeling. Eomer was a man of action, and now he could do nothing while his sister lay perhaps dying just feet away. Aragorn, however, focused all his attention on his patient; it was as if Eowyn was the only person in the entire world while he was tending her. I felt a pang in my heart that he would never turn that intensity of attention on me, then cursed myself for a self-centred fool when I realised where my thoughts had led me. How could I be jealous of a young woman who lay on the edge of death? I blamed fatigue from the battle and the incessant ache in my shoulder for my weakness and resolutely shifted my mind elsewhere.


A distraction soon presented itself. A commotion at the door brought my attention to a Gondorian soldier, who strode in with a small body in his arms. A shorter figure, also dressed in the livery of Minas Tirith, followed on his heels and it took me a moment to register that it was Pippin. The hobbit rushed forwards when he caught sight of Aragorn.


“It's Merry!” he cried. “I found him on the battlefield. He's hurt – please help him!”


Aragorn rose at once, not forgetting to give Eomer a reassuring pat on the shoulder as he did so, and crossed hurriedly to the pallet where the soldier was laying down his small burden. I knew there was nothing I could do to help, and thought I would only get in the way if I went to join them, so I remained where I was, praying with all my heart that Merry was not mortally injured. After everything we had been through, I could not bear the thought that one of the hobbits should lose his life in a battle on the other side of Middle Earth from his home. War is always an ugly thing, and has very little justice in it, but I couldn't believe in a world where innocence could be blighted so irrevocably.


I felt tears burning my eyes, so I clenched them shut, unwilling to lose my composure completely in these strange surroundings. I thought I only sat there like that for a moment, but I must have drifted into a doze. I was roused by a hand on my uninjured shoulder and opened my eyes to see Aragorn standing over me.


“Legolas,” he murmured. “How long have you been sitting here?”


I glanced around, slightly disoriented. “I'm not sure. I came to make sure you got some rest, but clearly I've not been fulfilling that duty.”


He sat down heavily on the bench next to me, clearly exhausted. “Well, I appreciate the thought. I think I've finally done all I can here, so I'll happily fulfil your duty for you by taking myself to bed.”


“How is Eowyn?” I wanted to know first.


“The bravest woman I've ever known,” was his comment. “Do you know she took on the Witch King and won?”


I gaped at him. An image sprang into my mind of the leader of the Nazgul looming over the slender shieldmaiden, and I was amazed she was still in one piece. I had never underestimated her strength of spirit, but such a feat was beyond anything I could imagine.


“Will she recover?” I asked.


Aragorn passed a hand across his face, as if trying to wipe the last few hours from his mind. “Given time, yes. Her wound was grievous, and almost beyond my ability to heal, but she is determined and she has the love of her brother to keep her anchored. She will be weak for a long time but she will recover fully.”


I breathed a little more easily, but there was still one friend to enquire about.


“And Merry? I saw him brought in. What happened to him?”


Aragorn smiled in wonder. “He was Eowyn's partner on the battlefield, of all things. He suffered a wound at the hand of the Witch King as well, trying to defend her from its attack. He is very lucky; his injury is not so severe and he, too, has love to anchor him. If Pippin hadn't found him out there among the dead, he wouldn't be alive now. Pippin hasn't left his side; they're both asleep over there on the floor. I wouldn't be surprised to find Merry right as rain by the morning.” He caught my eye. “Speaking of sleeping, that's what both of us should be doing. This isn't over yet, and we need to take advantage of the respite while we can.”


He rose and took hold of my arm as if to pull me up from the bench. I hissed at the pain as he jolted my arm, and his expression immediately turned to one of concern.


“Legolas, are you injured? Why didn't you say something? How bad is it?”


I couldn't help but smile as I realised he had turned all his focus instantly to my wellbeing, exactly as I had been wishing earlier on. I had been stupid not to realise that he would, and I basked in his friendship for a moment before coming back to my senses.


“I think I've dislocated my shoulder, that's all,” I reassured him. “There were many others in far greater need than me, so I didn't want to trouble you earlier.”


Aragorn sat down again and turned to face me, reaching up to probe my shoulder joint through the material of my tunic. I sucked in a breath sharply as his fingers caused the ache to flare, but managed to remain still.


“Yes, it's dislocated,” he said after a moment. Then he shifted and put one arm around my chest while the other rested against my injured arm. He looked deeply into my eyes and continued, “On the count of three, I'm going to push the joint back into place. Ready?”


I nodded, losing myself in his gaze as he sat inches from me, my pain forgotten for the moment.


“One, two -” Barely on two, he jerked against me and I felt my shoulder realign itself and explode into fiery agony. Aragorn held me to him as the spasm passed, leaving me breathless. When I looked up, there was an apology in his eyes.


“It hurts a lot less when you're not expecting it,” he explained.


“Now, to bed for both of us, I think,” he declared, getting to his feet once more. “I know how quickly you heal, so you'll probably be fine by tomorrow, as well, but sleep can do nothing but help.”


One of the healers' attendants showed us to available rooms and I was asleep within minutes.


* * * * *


The next morning came far too quickly. I realised as I washed and dressed how easy it had become for me to sleep after a battle and the change worried me a little. I knew it would do nobody any good for me to be exhausted after a sleepless night, but I didn't like the fact that I was turning into a soldier. I didn't want to be one of those hardened veterans who wasn't affected by violence and atrocity any more. Could it be possible that I was losing some of my feeling, in order to survive the battles emotionally as well as physically? I hoped not.


I had little time to dwell on these thoughts, as Gimli strode into my room without knocking.


“Aren't you ready yet?” he asked grumpily. “Everyone's waiting in the throne room.”


“Where did you get to last night?” I enquired as I fastened the last of my buckles.


“Straight to sleep for me,” he replied. “Which is what you should have done. Don't tell me you stayed up half the night babysitting Aragorn.”


I couldn't help but smile at his contempt. “That was my plan, but I fell asleep on duty,” I admitted.


I told Gimli briefly about Eowyn and the hobbits, interpreting his various grunts as expressions of concern and relief over the wellbeing of our friends. Then, boots on, I followed him out of the room.


As we walked down the corridor, I swung my arm back and forth a couple of times to test my shoulder. It still ached, but at least I had full use of my arm, and I thought it wouldn't hinder me too much if more fighting was required today. I had to admit I was as intrigued as I was apprehensive about the upcoming meeting, as I had no idea what the overall situation was, or what our next move would be.


Gandalf, Aragorn and Eomer were gathered in the throne room when we arrived. Aragorn looked worn but alert, which I hope meant he had managed to get at least as much sleep as me. Eomer's presence suggested that Eowyn was recovered enough for him to be happy to leave her side, which made me glad. Gimli stumped straight past everyone and sat himself down in the steward's chair without ceremony, fumbling for his pipe and lighting it as the debate began. I took up a position on the main floor, supporting my injured shoulder with my other arm.


Gandalf looked grave. “Frodo has passed beyond my sight. The darkness is deepening.”


This was not what I wanted to hear the morning after a major victory for our side, but of course the larger task of destroying the Ring was still far from over. In fact, as Gandalf pointed out, we had no idea of the status of Frodo and Sam, though Aragorn immediately stated the other side of the case.


“If Sauron had the Ring, we would know it,” he said.


“It's only a matter of time,” Gandalf countered, pessimistically. “He has suffered a defeat, yes, but behind the walls of Mordor our enemy is regrouping.”


“Let him stay there,” Gimli said forcefully. “Let him rot! Why should we care?”


Gandalf turned to face him, his expression stern. “Because ten thousand orcs now stand between Frodo and Mount Doom. I have sent him to his death.”


I had never heard the wizard – our guide and mentor – sound so negative, and it frightened me. Gandalf had never been less than realistic, but this was fatalism, an attitude we could not afford to indulge in, if we were to maintain our resistance against Sauron. Luckily, Aragorn seemed to have stepped in to fill the role of motivator in Gandalf's stead. Where Gandalf was being weakened by his guilt over the fate of Frodo, Aragorn had been bolstered by his experience with the ghost army and subsequent saving of so many people in the battle the day before.


“No,” he said firmly. “There is still hope for Frodo. He needs time, and safe passage across the plains of Gorgorath. We can give him that.”


“How?” Gimli wanted to know, his tone suggesting an eagerness to act. I was glad he was responding to Aragorn's determination rather than Gandalf's pessimism, as I knew he was prone to bouts of frustration and despair, like any of us.


“Draw out Sauron's armies,” was Aragorn's plan. “Empty his lands. Then we gather our full strength and march on the Black Gate.”


Gimli choked on his pipe smoke at this pronouncement, and I admit I felt myself quail at the thought of such an audacious move. After the battle of the day before, the last thing I wanted to do was march against vastly superior forces in what was sure to be suicide.


Eomer spoke for all of us. “We cannot achieve victory through strength of arms.”


It seemed that Aragorn was well aware of this, and it did not change his thoughts. “Not for ourselves,” he admitted, “but we can give Frodo his chance if we keep Sauron's eye fixed upon us – keep him blind to all else that moves.”


“A diversion,” I summarised, seeing now where his thoughts had led him. It seemed now that he had found his kingdom, he was willing to sacrifice it in order to save the world as a whole. I did not envy him the decision, but I was prepared, as always, to support and follow him to the very end.


Gimli, too, was not dissuaded. “Certainty of death, small chance of success. What are we waiting for?” His tone was sarcastic, but I knew he, too, would follow where Aragorn led.


Gandalf was yet to be swayed. “Sauron will suspect a trap. He will not take the bait.”


“Oh, I think he will,” Aragorn said, his face set in grim lines.


Without another word, he strode through an opening into the inner sanctum of the citadel. The others exchanged uncertain glances, but I followed Aragorn, stopping in the doorway to see him uncovering a Palantir. It blazed with an inner light and he flinched away from it momentarily. I could see him steel himself for a confrontation, his back tensing. Then he seized the Palantir and held it up, looking straight into its depths.


“Long have you hunted me,” Aragorn announced to his enemy. “Long have I eluded you. No more.”


With that, he brought up his newly forged sword and presented it to whatever or whoever looked out from the other side of the connected Palantirs. Aragorn stood motionless for a long moment, his entire body betraying the strain of whatever mental struggle was taking place.


This was the future king of Gondor in full force, meeting his enemy head on and showing no fear. But suddenly, he faltered, lowering the sword haltingly, his shoulders slumped. The Palantir dropped from his hand back onto the platform and he covered it quickly with a shudder. When he turned, his expression was haunted, anxiety etched into his face.


I closed the distance between us rapidly. “What is it? What did you see?” I asked him.


Aragorn looked up at me wearily. “The enemy is coming,” he replied. “He looked into my mind and attacked me where I am most vulnerable. He showed me Arwen. She’s dying, Legolas! And I’m so far away from her.”


I took hold of his upper arms, grounding him and pulling him away from his vision.


“Arwen is strong,” I told him. “She may have given up immortality but she has many years of life still ahead of her. When this is all over, you will be together at last.”


But sudden despair still clouded Aragorn’s mind, blocking him from the determination he had demonstrated only moments before. “We have defeated his armies twice, yet Sauron still threatens us. Will this ever be over?”


My unshakable faith in him allowed me to answer with certainty. “Yes. We have won victory over seemingly impossible odds on two separate occasions now. We will defeat Sauron and you will rule over Gondor in justice and peace.”


Renewed determination flashed in Aragorn’s eyes and he spoke firmly. “I am ready now to accept that destiny,” he said, but then deflated again. “But it is hard sometimes to be strong all the time.”


In response, I pulled him into my embrace and held him to me. “I remember a young boy who cried in frustration because he could not run as fast as a certain elven princeling,” I murmured in his ear. “And I remember a youth who wrote dreadful love poetry after laying eyes on Elrond’s daughter for the first time.” I pushed him back to arm’s length and caught his gaze. “You do not have to be strong with me.”


Aragorn searched my face with troubled eyes. “Why do you follow me, Legolas?”


“Because I would not be parted from you,” I told him, echoing Eowyn’s words at Helm’s Deep, the pain of honesty constricting around my heart, the time to speak plainly finally upon me after so long. “Because I love you.”


Aragorn reached up and traced a finger down my cheek, his touch leaving a trail of fire across my skin. When he spoke, his tone was full of sorrow.


“I cannot give you what you seek,” he said, just as he had done to Eowyn.


I took his hand in mine, pulling it back down.


“I do not seek anything from you, my friend,” I said sincerely. “I give of my love freely, and with no expectation of return.”


“Whatever love I have left to offer is yours,” he replied softly.


I smiled then, touched by his open affection. “I know. Now, I think we have a battle to join.”


We both took a moment to compose ourselves, then I followed him back out into the main room where the others still waited.


“We must prepare ourselves once again to face our enemy, my friends,” Aragorn announced. “We march in one hour.”


* * * * *


Since I had little preparation to do and no men to muster, I went back to the Houses of Healing to check on the wounded. Eowyn was sleeping, her expression peaceful, but I found the two hobbits causing quite a ruckus. They appeared to be having a pillow fight.


“Legolas!” Pippin cried as soon as he spotted me. “It's so good to see you!”


“As it is to see you,” I replied wholeheartedly, smiling at his animation.


Merry was just as enthusiastic in his greeting, bounding up from his bed to greet me.


It was at this point that one of the healing attendants approached. “Can you please take these two out of here? They're disturbing the other patients, and there's really nothing wrong with either one of them.”


“Truly?” I queried. “Merry is quite recovered? That is good news indeed.”


“Yes, yes,” she replied. “But if he and his friend stay here, they may well suffer further injury at my hand. So, please, take them away.”


I laughed and turned back to the hobbits. “You heard the lady. You've outstayed your welcome here. Come along with me, and we'll see if we can find more appropriate rooms for you.”


They followed me out of the Houses of Healing meekly enough, but burst into frenzied questions as soon as we got out into the corridor.


“What's going on?” Pippin demanded. “Have you heard anything of Frodo or Sam?”


“Is the fighting really over now?” Merry wanted to know. “Have we won yet?”


I held up my hands to silence them.


“Not yet,” I replied seriously. “In fact, we are marching on the Black Gate within the hour in order to provide a distraction. Aragorn believes it will allow Frodo and Sam to reach Mount Doom and end this once and for all.”


I knew as soon as the words left my lips that I should never have spoken them. Indeed, the hobbits immediately demanded to be part of the diversionary expedition. I tried to reason with them but they were adamant.


“Frodo and Sam are our friends,” Merry pointed out. “We've come this far – we have to follow this through. We can't be left behind at the end.”


“Just last night, you were lying at death's door,” I reminded him. “Do you really want to go straight back out into battle?”


Merry sobered, the light in his eyes dimming as he recalled his experiences of the day before. “Do you?” he asked me solemnly.


I was reminded of my thoughts of earlier that morning, of how much I hated the changes that had been wrought in me over the many months I had been a part of this war.


“Of course not,” I said quietly. “But it must be done if we are ever to come out from under Sauron's shadow. I am well aware that I may not live out this day, but I am prepared to make that sacrifice to safeguard Middle Earth.”


“As are we,” Pippin stated firmly, Merry nodding beside him. “Don't stop us from doing our part. We've both proved now that we can fight, and it sounds like every sword will count. We want to come too.”


Much as I wanted to protect them, I could not argue with them on this point. They were right; they deserved the opportunity to see this through to its conclusion. I would not be able to remain behind if it were my childhood friends out on the other side of that wall, so how could I deny them the chance to offer their assistance?


I waited while they found and put on their armour, marvelling at how much they looked like seasoned soldiers when they were finished. Pippin stood in his uniform of black and silver, the White Tree of Gondor shining on his chest, while Merry looked determined in the green and yellow of Rohan, two miniature warriors girding themselves for battle. My heart constricted as I looked at them, but I could not fault their determination.


I led them to the assembly point outside the city. Glances of surprise and disapproval were thrown my way by Gimli and Gandalf as we approached, but I stared them both down and neither said a word as the hobbits presented themselves to join our party. Gandalf pulled Pippin up behind him on Shadowfax, while Eomer knelt before Merry.


“I am sorry I doubted you, Meriadoc Brandybuck,” he said formally, “and I can never repay the debt I owe you for your protection of my sister. Tell me what service I may do you, and I will do it.”


“Take me into battle with you,” Merry replied immediately with equal seriousness.


“I would be honoured to have you at my back,” Eomer said.


He swung into his saddle and pulled Merry up behind him.


Gimli had magicked up Arod from somewhere, so we once again rode together among our friends.


Aragorn was resplendent in a Gondorian tunic, boldly declaring his allegiance to the city and taking claim of his authority over the army. The troops formed up behind him and he led us away from the city and towards the dark place on the horizon where all our fates would be decided.


We rode slowly, not wishing to outpace the infantry soldiers behind us, but we still arrived at the Black Gates of Mordor far sooner than I would have thought possible. My heart hammered in my chest at being so close to Sauron's lair and I wondered, not for the first time, if I would ever leave this place. Gimli's solid presence at my back reassured me and I knew we would fight and die together if that was what the day held in store for us.


The army halted a little way away from the gate itself, and I could feel the awe and fear of the soldiers as they looked up at its forbidding menace.


Aragorn took his horse out in front and called out a challenge. “Let the Lord of the Black Land come forth! Let justice be done upon him!”


A moment later, the gate opened a few feet and a single figure on a horse emerged. It was swathed in a long black cloak and had a massive helmet that covered the majority of its face. Its mouth, however, remained in view and it grinned across the distance between us, revealing hideous, yellowed teeth. When it spoke, its voice was oily and sent shudders down my spine, as if I had suddenly been covered in thick, black tar.


“My master, Sauron the Great, bids thee welcome,” it said. “Is there any in this rout with authority to treat with me?”


Gandalf spoke up on our behalf. “We do not come to treat with Sauron, faithless and accursed. Tell your master this: the armies of Mordor must disband. He is to depart these lands, never to return.”


I do not know what response Gandalf expected to this pronouncement, but it did not surprise me when the creature grinned again, apparently completely unaffected by the wizard's demands.


“Aha, Old Greybeard. I have a token I was bidden to show thee,” it said, pulling something out from under its cloak.


I instantly recognised the mithril shirt Frodo had been wearing when he was stabbed by the cave troll under Moria. It seemed like years had passed since that encounter, back when we were all still together, before we had lost Boromir and been separated from Frodo and Sam.


I heard Pippin whisper Frodo's name despairingly, as the creature at the gate threw the shirt to Gandalf, who snatched it out of the air and clutched it to him almost as if it were actually Frodo's body and not just a garment.


The evil creature laughed, but Gandalf shouted, “Silence!”


Merry cried out in denial of what the shirt represented, but Gandalf called for silence once more and the hobbit grew still.


“The halfling was dear to thee, I see,” the creature continued, taunting us. “Know that he suffered greatly at the hands of his host. Who would have thought one so small could endure so much pain? And he did, Gandalf. He did.” It chuckled at its own words and I itched to have my hands around its neck.


Gandalf passed the shirt to Pippin, whose face betrayed his horror and sorrow at this discussion of his friend.


Aragorn, meanwhile, spurred his horse forward to confront the creature.


“And who is this?” it asked contemptuously. “Isildur's heir? It takes more to make a king than a broken Elvish blade.”


Before it could say another word, Aragorn brandished that very blade and lopped its head off.


“I guess that concludes negotiations,” Gimli said wryly from behind me.


Aragorn turned back to his troops. “I do not believe it. I will not,” he said.


Behind him, the gates opened once more, this time not stopping but swinging wide. I heard the shouts of orcs emanating from within the walls, and suddenly they began to swarm out onto the plain.


“Fall back! Fall back!” Aragorn cried, and we all rode back to rejoin the soldiers.


Aragorn rode along the front line. “Hold your ground!” he called out. “Hold your ground! Sons of Gondor, of Rohan! My brothers! I see in your eyes the same fear that would take the heart of me! A day may come when the courage of men fails, when we forsake our friends and break all bonds of fellowship. But it is not this day. An hour of wolves and shattered shields, when the age of men comes crashing down. But it is not this day! This day we fight! By all that you hold dear on this good earth. I bid you stand, Men of the West!”


I felt my heart rise to meet his passion and I marvelled at his leadership, which might just hold us together long enough to allow Frodo the time he needed to finish his quest. All the solders drew their weapons and held themselves at the ready to meet the enemy.


We watched as the orcs streamed out from the gates and started to circle around us.


“Quickly!” Aragorn called to those of us on horses. “Dismount and send the horses back towards the city. We will not be able to use them at such close quarters, without risking our own men.”


We followed his instructions and joined the soldiers on the ground. The horses galloped away as if their tails were on fire, surely knowing they were escaping certain death. The orcs quickly surrounded us completely, but kept their distance at first, corralling us and taunting us but not yet attacking.


Gimli stood beside me, his axe in his hand. “Never thought I'd die fighting side by side with an elf,” he joked, harking back to our first meeting and its near disastrous animosity.


“What about side by side with a friend?” I countered, not wanting to start this battle with flippancy.


“Aye, I could do that,” he replied.


It was at the moment, surrounded by orcs and facing almost certain death, that a realisation came to me. I had been wasting my life in the hopes of achieving something that would never come to be. I knew in my heart of hearts that Aragorn would never be mine, and I had thought I was satisfied with loving him from afar and accepting the friendship that was all he could give. But I was fooling myself and not paying attention to what was happening around me. My selfish, self-pitying feelings were so unimportant and so ridiculous when compared to the bond that had been forged between myself and those who stood around me; a wizard I would forever look up to with awe and affection, two young hobbits who had stolen their way into my heart when I wasn't looking, and a grumbling dwarf who had become as dear to me as my own family back in Mirkwood. These were real feelings, emotions that had meaning in the midst of this struggle, bonds that would be with me for the rest of my life, be that many years or merely minutes.


I looked across at Aragorn, standing ready at the head of his troops, and it struck me that the ideal of him that I loved was not a real person, but merely something I had constructed out of adolescent fancy and boyish dreams. The Aragorn who stood on that field ready to face his enemies once again was not a perfect creature of pure romantic love; he was a man, with weaknesses that would need to be bolstered by my support. He did not need my love, he needed my loyalty and my strength to help him through the trial we faced together. The same was true of those dear friends who stood with us, and I knew each of them would do the same for me. This was real, this was important and it deserved my undivided focus. I would dream no more.


Meanwhile, we were at a stand-off with the orc army. They shrieked and gestured at us but made no move to attack. I wondered what they were waiting for, but didn't have a chance to find out when they would have broken the uneasy inactivity.


Aragorn turned to look over his shoulder at us and said, “For Frodo.”


With that, he brandished his sword and started running towards the orcs. Merry and Pippin immediately ran after him, yelling at the tops of their lungs, with myself and Gimli right behind them. I heard the soldiers joining the charge and then we were upon the enemy, smashing through their line and beginning the battle on our terms.


And so I found myself knee-deep in orcs for the second time in as many days, this time without supernatural allies to help. I couldn't help thinking Gimli had had the right idea the day before; we should have kept the ghost army available to us as long as possible. Aragorn would never have stood for that, though. So, for better or worse, we were on our own this time.


I tried to keep an eye on my friends, particularly the hobbits, but it was impossible in the crush of the battle. The orcs were literally all around us, and it was a struggle just to remain upright, most of the time, let alone watch out for the tiny figures of Merry and Pippin. I would have to just trust that their luck would hold for one more fight. Gimli started out at my side, but we were soon separated, and I lost sight of Gandalf's whirling staff early on, as well. I wasn't counting victims this time; I needed all my concentration to stay alive.


When the Nazgul arrived on their flying monsters, I thought it was all over. I heard the screeching overhead and felt a wave of fear and despair wash through the men around me. I didn't need to look up to know they were there, which was a good thing, since taking my eye off my opponent would surely have resulted in my death. I just waited in dread to feel teeth or claws in the back of my neck; I figured it was only a matter of time.


However, a familiar voice lifted my heart, not only because of the words it spoke, but also because it confirmed that at least one of my friends was still alive.


“Eagles! The eagles are coming!” Pippin's cry rose up above the noise of the battle, and my eyes were inevitably drawn upwards to see our giant winged allies join the fray against the Nazgul.


Safe in the knowledge now that death would not come from above, I wrenched my attention back to the ground just in time to avoid a sword thrust from an orc, and continued the endless battle. Some time later, I was aware in the periphery of my vision that the Nazgul were retreating, and I heard a ragged cheer go up from my comrades. We needed every morale boost we could get at that point, so it was a very welcome sight indeed.


Another familiar cry caught my attention and this time it was not so heartening. I looked across the battlefield to see Aragorn struggling against an enormous troll. As I watched, the creature landed a blow that sent Aragorn flying and then it advanced on his motionless form, evidently preparing to finish the job. I started running towards them, stabbing orcs out of my way as I went, but I knew I would never get there in time.


The troll bore down on Aragorn and raised its mighty club to crush him, but Aragorn managed to bring his knife round to stab it before it could complete its swing. I was still several feet away, when I felt a wave of shock and anger sweep through me from an unknown source. I staggered at the intensity of the emotion, and my eyes blurred for a moment. When my vision cleared, I looked up to see that all of Sauron's forces had stopped fighting. As I looked on in surprise, the troll who had been battling Aragorn suddenly looked back towards Mordor and started running from the battlefield. All of its comrades followed suit, abandoning the battle.


I closed the distance to Aragorn and helped him to his feet, checking as I did so that he was unhurt.


Then, my eyes were drawn to the tower of Barad-Dur and the great Eye atop it, which seemed to be disoriented, spinning its gaze without coherence. Everyone's eyes were fixed on it as its light dimmed and the tower started to crumble. As the stone toppled, the Eye gradually shrank in size until it was completely distinguished. At the moment it disappeared, a ring of light burst from its source, sending an accompanying shock wave sweeping over the fleeing army. The land around the Black Gate began to collapse in on itself, taking the gate and many of the orcs and trolls with it. They screamed as they fell into the abyss opening up beneath their feet, and I feared we would all suffer the same fate. However, the destruction halted just shy of where our troops were milling around in confusion, leaving us still on solid ground.


I caught sight of Merry's grinning face in the throng. “Frodo! Frodo!” he cried in triumph, surmising as I had that the young hobbit had at last managed to cast the Ring into the fires of Mount Doom. As if to corroborate Merry's thought, the mountain itself suddenly exploded, expelling lava and rock into the sky.


Pippin repeated Merry's call of Frodo's name but, this time, he was crying. If Frodo had been in the mountain when it erupted, it was unlikely he would have survived.


Gandalf, however, was not ready to give up. He crossed to where the eagles had landed a short distance away and climbed up onto one feathery back.


“If they are alive, we will find them,” he called to us and, in moments, the eagles had taken flight and were winging their way towards the mountain. We waited, unable to voice our fears or acknowledge our hopes, our eyes on the the sky as the eagles shrank to specks. I clutched at Aragorn's arm and his answering grip told me his anxiety matched my own.


At long last, I saw the eagles returning and struggled to make out what it was that they carried in their claws. Eventually, the tiny forms of Sam and Frodo came into focus, and I saw Gandalf waving from atop the lead eagle.


I looked around, grinning, but then realised that no-one else could yet make out the returning eagles.


“The eagles have Frodo and Sam!” I called out. “Gandalf is waving so they must still be alive.”


A huge cheer went up as the eagles flew overhead, aiming for Minas Tirith and safety at long last for those two heroic hobbits. I was nearly knocked from my feet by Merry and Pippin hurtling to embrace me and then each other, and I looked round to see Aragorn grinning at my side. A hand on my arm brought my attention down to Gimli, who was standing at my side with tears in his eyes. I almost started crying myself with the knowledge that all my friends were safe and whole, and that the battle was finally over, once and for all.


Together, we started on our weary way back towards the city to be reunited with the saviours of Middle Earth.


* * * * *


When we reached Minas Tirith and climbed our way up to the citadel, Gandalf met us at the main entrance. He forestalled the questions of Merry and Pippin before they could even open their mouths.


“Frodo and Sam are both alive and safe. Your friends are back with us at last. Come, I will take you to them.”


Aragorn touched Gandalf's arm. “Do they need my help?”


“No, they just need rest and the love of their friends,” the wizard reassured him. “You go and rest, too. I think you've done your part for today. The hobbits will still be there for you to fuss over in the morning.” He gave Aragorn a fatherly smile and went inside. Merry, Pippin and Gimli followed him.


Aragorn looked about ready to collapse. I took his arm and led him inside, finding his room more by luck than memory. Once inside, he turned to me, a whole range of emotions etched onto his face. It looked as though he didn't know whether to feel happy or sad at the situation we now found ourselves in.


He sank down onto the bed and looked up at me, his eyes lost and searching for reassurance.


“Is it really over, Legolas?” he asked me. “Really and truly, have we defeated Sauron at long last?”


“Yes, my friend,” I told him softly. “We really have.”


As if he had needed someone else to say the words out loud for him to believe it, Aragorn's face crumpled and he dissolved into helpless tears, finally allowing himself to let go of the tension he must have been holding inside all this time.


I crossed to the bed and sat down next to him, taking him in my arms and holding him to my chest as he cried. I felt him shudder against me and felt nothing more than compassion for the sense of responsibility he must have felt so strongly since the very beginning of our journey together, and even before. This was a truly great man I held in my arms and I felt privileged that he was comfortable enough in my company to reveal to me his human weakness and true feelings.


I let him cry, rocking him gently and whispering words of comfort in Elvish. Eventually, his tears ceased and he sat up, his gaze dropping to his hands as if he was ashamed to look at me.


“What kind of behaviour is this for the heir to Gondor?” he said softly, half-smiling at himself.


I laid my hand on his shoulder, and he raised his eyes to meet mine.


“I told you before, that is not who you are to me,” I said. “Even kings need to shed their crowns at times, and I am glad to be a friend you can turn to in such times of need. Promise me you will never let titles or decorum come between us.”


He smiled. “I believe that is a promise I can keep,” he said. “Thank you, Legolas. You have no idea how much your friendship means to me.” His face grew serious again. “But what about you? I can offer you the same, but I know that is not what you want from me. How can that be fair?”


“It is now all I want from you,” I told him honestly. “And I will accept your unconditional friendship gladly. Elven princelings sometimes need to shed their reserve as well.” He smiled at my flippancy. “You do not need to worry about my heart. It is whole and healthy, and just realising there are many things in my life that need a share in its attention. I will not be pining away, never fear.”


Before he could answer that, I rose and continued lightly. “Now it is time for us both to get some sleep. There are going to be four hobbits running around here tomorrow, so we're going to need our energy.”


That raised a laugh. “Goodnight, Legolas.”


“Goodnight, Aragorn.”


* * * * *


The following morning, I found out from one of the servants where Frodo and Sam had been taken, and made my way towards their rooms. On the way, I inevitably came across Merry, Pippin, Gimli and Aragorn, also on their way to visit with our long-lost friends.


“They were both asleep when we stopped by last night,” Gimli told me, “but Gandalf said they should both be up and about today.


As if summoned by his words, a door opened nearby and Sam emerged into the corridor. Merry and Pippin flung themselves at him and enveloped him in a three-way hug. He looked a little startled at first, but submitted to their attentions with a quiet smile on his face. He greeted the rest of us as well, and I saw a great weariness in his eyes as they met mine. I looked back at him, trying to convey my appreciation of what he had done and my understanding that he was no longer the same hobbit I had parted from in the wilderness. He held my gaze for a long moment, and then nodded, as if accepting my tacit support.


Merry and Pippin released him and then raced ahead and into a different room. There were exclamations of joy, and the rest of us followed more slowly, to see the two young hobbits bouncing on a huge bed, which also contained Frodo.


The hero of the hour was sitting up in bed, smiling down at his cousins as they rejoiced in their reunion. Like Sam, he seemed somehow apart from them, a shadow across his face, which told of his many trials. He called out our names as he noticed us in the doorway, and seemed happy to see us, but I sensed there were many things he yet had to recover from. He was not the same, either, and I didn't think he ever would be. When Sam eventually crossed the threshold, a look passed between them that told me nobody else would ever understand what they had been through together. I was glad they had each other, though.


Gandalf had already been seated in the room when we arrived, and so it was that the eight remaining members of our fellowship came together again at long last.


* * * * *


We all spent a few days simply coming to terms with the fact that we did not have to journey elsewhere to carry on the fight. The citizens of Minas Tirith worked hard to clear the debris of the battle, both masonry and casualties, and to move on with their lives. Aragorn leaped straight into his new role as leader of the city, organising the clearing crews and going out into the streets himself to help. Gimli and I joined them, too, when we weren't spending time with the hobbits. Frodo gained strength daily, but still remained slightly remote. Merry and Pippin didn't seem to notice, being boisterous enough for four hobbits just on their own, but I saw Sam looking at Frodo with sorrow in his eyes more than once.


One afternoon, I was standing at the far end of the promontory at the top of the city, looking out across the plains to where the sky was gradually clearing over Mordor. What once had been a blight on both my vision and my soul was now the most reassuring view available to me, confirming as it did that Sauron was really gone. I heard footsteps behind me and turned to find Sam approaching.


“Good afternoon,” I said in welcome.


He said nothing as he joined me at the wall, looking out at Mordor as I had been a moment before. I waited, sure he would speak when he was ready, or willing just to share the view with him in silence if that was his preference.


He did speak after a few minutes.


“What do you feel?” he asked suddenly, his voice solemn and so quiet I had to strain to hear him.


I took a moment to consider the question, trying to fathom what he was asking me.


Eventually, I found some words to respond. “I feel sorrow at the suffering I have seen, and relief that it is now at an end. I feel determination to help rebuild Middle Earth so that it can be what it once was. I feel anger and shame at the things I have been forced to do and endure. I feel regret for all the things I could not prevent, and for all the things my friends suffered without me there to help them. Most of all, I feel sad that I cannot just go back to being who I was – carefree and innocent of all this. Soon, I will go home and carry on with my life, but I will never again be the same, and I feel fear that nobody there will understand that.”


As my speech came to a close, I looked down to see tears falling on Sam's cheeks.


He still did not look at me, but his choked reply reached my ears all the same. “That is what I feel, too.”


“So, now you know that there others who will understand, even if those at home do not. I believe that, no matter how far we may be separated, we will always be there for each other. This journey we have faced will bind us together forever.” I laid a hand on his shoulder and he finally looked up at me. “The rest of us will never know all that you and Frodo went through on your part of the quest, and I know he will need you to support and comfort him for a long time to come. You are his anchor, the only one who has any chance of understanding his suffering. But that does not mean you cannot look for comfort of your own. Know that you can always send a message to Mirkwood and I will come to you.”


“Thank you,” Sam said, his voice barely above a whisper.


Then he turned back to the view and we stood there together, looking out over the field of our final victory, until the descending sun sent us back inside for warmth and more lively companionship.


* * * * *


A few days later, the same space Sam and I had shared was packed with people present for Aragorn's coronation. He had requested that the ceremony be as short and informal as possible, as always not wanting all the attention to rest on him. Gandalf and Gimli stood on the citadel steps with him, an honour I had declined but refused to tell him why. I had another place in the ceremony that I hoped would act as the pleasantest surprise of the day.


The coronation itself consisted simply of Gandalf placing the winged Gondorian crown on Aragorn's head. He then stood back to reveal the new king to his people. Aragorn had let his beard grow out and now it added distinction to his face. He was dressed in full kingly regalia and looked magnificent, gazing out over the crowd.


Gandalf announced, “Now come the days of the king. May they be blessed.”


Aragorn himself turned away and made his way to the very top of the steps, turning once more to address his subjects. There was a great cheer.


“This day does not belong to one man,” he said in his usual self-deprecating way, “but to all. Let us together rebuild this world, that we may share in the days of peace.”


Cheering erupted once more, as flower petals descended from the white tower to cover the crowd. Aragorn descended the steps once more and went out into the crowd to meet his people. I watched him move among them and rejoiced that he had at last found a place in the world where he could do good and be appreciated fully by those around him. I knew without a doubt that he would make a good king.


Eowyn was near the front of the crowd and one of the first to present herself to the newly crowned king. By her side stood Faramir, the steward of the city, whom I had met the day after the last battle. I smiled to see them so happy with each other, and it made me glad to know that Eowyn had found love. I felt a kinship to her and wished her well in her new life.


Shortly afterwards, Aragorn approached where I was standing with the other elves who had made the trip to witness the coronation. He stopped in front of me and pulled me into a joyous embrace.


“Thank you,” he said in Elvish, and I knew he meant for more than just my presence there that day.


I had one more thing to give him. Smiling, I stepped to one side to reveal Elrond and Arwen standing behind me. The look on Aragorn's face as he laid eyes on his beloved was worth a thousand thanks. I don't think I have ever been so happy as I was in that moment when I reunited them after so long a separation. Aragorn looked back at me and I knew he saw only happiness in my face, no regret shadowing my joy. He smiled his gratitude and then turned back to Arwen. She stepped forward, carrying a banner which portrayed the White Tree of Gondor, symbolising her commitment to him in his new role as king. He raised her chin and looked deep into her eyes, neither of them speaking or moving. Then, suddenly, he swept her into his arms and kissed her hard, taking her by surprise and delighting the surrounding crowd, who burst once more into cheers and applause. Elrond also smiled, finally accepting his daughter's choice.


Aragorn and Arwen proceeded through the crowd together, with the rest of us following behind. They came to a halt before a line of four hobbits, who all bowed together.


“My friends!” Aragorn called to forestall them. “You bow to no-one.”


With that, he knelt before them, Arwen joining him and the rest of the crowd gradually following suit. The hobbits looked distinctly uncomfortable at this show of appreciation, but they deserved it after all they had done for us. I felt privileged to call them my friends.


After that, the crowd dissipated and Aragorn led Arwen, the hobbits, Elrond, Gandalf, Gimli, Eowyn, Faramir, Eomer and myself inside the citadel to join him for dinner. This was a lighthearted affair after the solemnity of the ceremony outside, but an event tinged with sadness. I knew that this was the last time all of us would be together like this and, despite all the suffering we had endured, it made me sad in a way to think that our journey had finally come to its conclusion. We had battled evil and won, and now we would depart to our homes. It seemed almost an anti-climax and also a miracle that we still had homes to go to. I looked around at the beloved faces sharing the table with me, and suddenly I knew that this was not the end, but in fact the beginning of a new chapter in all our lives. I wondered what fate had in store for each of us now, and I looked forward to finding out.
