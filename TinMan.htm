Tin Man

 

Writer: Jeff King

Director: Jimmy Kaufman

 

This ep opens on one of Jack’s sillier moods with his cabin crew impression.  I would have thought he’d generally be a bit more businesslike on arriving on a new planet, but hey.  Perhaps the MALP telemetry showed no activity around the gate for a long time.  Which brings me to something I thought of during Enigma and which applies here too.  I’ve always wondered how long they monitor the planets before sending a team through, and how long it is after sending a probe that people follow.  Sometimes they don’t seem to know what they’re going to see on the other side of the wormhole, as if they’ve not looked at the probe data and, other times, they’ve already made assumptions about the planet before they arrive.

 

Anyway, it’s a nice change for the gate to be inside a building rather than open grassland or the now familiar forest.  Here there are no trees for Jack to complain about!

 

It seems strange that the incapacitation happens so far away from the gate.  Is it triggered by Sam touching the computer?  This is not clear, but I would have thought it would be more sensible for this to happen immediately upon their arrival.  What if they’d gone in a different direction, for instance?  It’s possible Harlan was observing them remotely somehow and triggered the beam himself, I suppose – something we’ll never know!  I do like the way it takes Teal’c slightly longer to fall over than the others, demonstrating his greater strength and endurance.

 

They all look good in black, but the new outfit is especially yummy on Daniel – how did you guess I was going to say that? :o)

 

However, the way we are introduced to the “synthetic others” has always annoyed me.  It’s very clever, because we automatically assume they are the heroes we know and love, which makes us sympathetic to them before we find out the truth.  This means that we more easily accept that they have exactly the same memories and personalities as the real SG-1, which is why I don’t like it.  We care about them as we care about the real ones, and this makes the whole situation really sad.

 

I can very easily relate to Jack’s impatience and frustration, as I find Harlan intensely annoying, another reason I’m not so keen on this ep.  It also makes me feel even more sorry for the robots, who have to stay behind and live with the guy for all eternity.  Daniel is very sweet in the way he asks Harlan how long he’s been on his own, showing sympathy for his plight and understanding that this is why his interpersonal skills are a little rusty.

 

Question:  Harlan says “99,207,000 of your hours”, which suggests he measures time differently.  If this is the case, how does he know what one of “our hours” is?  Has he accessed SG-1’s memories during the transference?

 

There’s great Jack/Daniel contrast in their reactions to finding out that they are now artificial.  Daniel, of course, is totally fascinated, while Jack is angry and afraid, seeing the realistic problems with the situation rather than the scientific marvel.

 

Teal’c very nearly loses his feet when they fall back through the gate onto the station.  The way he bends his knees and lifts his feet away from the event horizon shows that the director was probably a bit worried about this too – and it is awfully close!

 

Even after Jack’s “reality check” and in a situation of great stress and imminent system meltdown, Daniel’s enthusiasm and fascination are irrepressible.  I love the way he stops to be impressed by his new strength when opening the vent, and Sam has to drag his attention back to the task in hand.

 

The scene where they discover their original selves has always really confused me.  The Real O’Neill asks Harlan where he’s been, suggesting that he knows who he is and has had conversations with him before.  In this case, I find it difficult to believe that SG-1 would allow Harlan to incapacitate them in the way they are when they are discovered.  And if they weren’t restrained willingly, they seem remarkably calm when they are released.

 

The issue of the robots having to give up their lives is only briefly dealt with in the conversation between Real O’Neill and Robot Jack, and not very satisfactorily, in my opinion.  All four of them have friends and family they will never be able to see again, it’s still Season One so Daniel should be extremely reluctant to give up on Sha’re, and there isn’t exactly an awful lot of things to do on the station.  The four robot characters are introduced, we are tricked into caring about them as if they were the real SG-1, and then they are just abandoned.  I know they reappear in Season Four – but that’s hardly any comfort, because then they all get killed!  I just think the whole situation is a little harsh…

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Malapropism:

Robot Jack: Colonel Jack O’Neill.  Kum-by-yah.

Harlan: Comtraya!

Robot Jack: Whatever.

 

Robot Jack makes sure we know he’s exactly like the Real O’Neill by indulging in his catchphrase a total of three times!

Janet: General, that is not Colonel O’Neill.

Robot Jack: Oh, for crying out loud – General, it’s me!

 

Robot Daniel: You’ve lost your infant Goa’uld.

Robot Teal’c: I still feel it’s presence.

Robot Daniel: Really!  That must be like continuing to feel a limb after it’s lost.

Robot Jack: Oh, for crying out loud.  Daniel!  I’m glad you’re so fascinated by this, but it’s time for a reality check, okay?

 

Harlan: [off screen] Colonel O’Neill!

Real O’Neill and Robot Jack together: Oh, for crying out loud…
