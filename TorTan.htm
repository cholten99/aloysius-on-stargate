Torment of Tantalus

 

Writer: Robert C Cooper

Director: Jonathan Glassner

 

The opening conversation between Jack and Daniel sets up their later conflict nicely.  Their contrasting personalities are effectively highlighted by the way Jack can’t understand why Daniel is interested in the old recordings, while Daniel can’t understand why Jack isn’t.

 

We have the re-introduction of Catherine, of course – I like this Catherine much better than the one in the film, as is the case with all the characters that cross over.  That’s probably because I’ve watched the series over and over and grown to love all the characters in it, while I’ve only seen the film once or twice.  Ordinarily, I don’t tend to like it when characters change from their original presentation in something, but Stargate is an exception, where I became a fan of the series without really equating it with the movie.

 

Daniel’s terrible dress sense becomes apparent for the first time.  He does much better on Abydos where he is restricted by traditional costume and on base where he has to wear his uniform.  When he’s allowed to choose his own clothes, he falls into the academic stereotype by failing to wear anything that matches!

 

He takes full advantage of his civilian status here by taking Catherine to the mountain without asking permission.  General Hammond clearly begins to realise that Daniel is just as much trouble as Jack, perhaps more since he has very little control over what Daniel does.  When all four member of SG-1 start trying to persuade him to let them go to Ernest’s planet, he doesn’t stand a chance – united, they present quite a formidable front!  However, Hammond gets his own back by telling them that Daniel’s original argument already won him over; I expect he rather enjoys letting them think he’s against their plan so that they have to keep coming up with arguments!

 

The way Jack and Daniel help Catherine through the event horizon is really sweet.  They may disagree on a lot of things, but they share thoughtfulness and a sense of chivalry.

 

As it turns out, Jack does respect Daniel’s abilities after all, turning to him in situations that require delicacy.  When Ernest appears completely naked and starts hugging them all, Jack’s response is to say, “Daniel, do something.”  He recognises that Daniel’s strengths lie in communication and people skills, and rightly encourages him to take the lead when appropriate.

 

This episode promotes the idea of a Sam/Daniel relationship more than anything else – the similarities between Daniel and Ernest, and between Sam and Catherine are clear (in the flashbacks, Catherine has ideas about the generators and Ernest obviously has great enthusiasm for ancient cultures).  I like the way Daniel goes to talk to Ernest while Sam provides a sounding board for Catherine’s reaction to the situation – the younger two are both very compassionate and good listeners.

 

Jack/Daniel contrast shows up again when attention turns to the DHD.  Daniel is a born teacher, launching into a lecture on the purpose of the DHD, while Jack suggests a demonstration in his straightforward, action-led manner.  Theory vs practice sums them up quite nicely.

 

Jack does exhibit signs of a brain here, though – despite his usual attempts to play dumb, he is the first to recognise the elements, and he also comes up with the idea of harnessing the lightning to power the gate.  It’s not often that Jack gets to come up with the solution to a problem but, in this episode, he is definitely firing on all cylinders!

 

Watching Daniel figure out that the representations of the elements are a universal language is really wonderful.  He gets so wrapped up in his fascination that he totally loses sight of everything else.  Why is it that obsessive personalities are so compelling, when they generally make the worst people with whom to have a relationship?  :o)

 

Jack cuts through Daniel’s enthusiasm with pragmatics again, expressing his realistic viewpoint with classic O’Neill eloquence: “None of which will mean squat if we don’t get out of here.”

 

He does understand Daniel’s passion, though – his expression when he comes with the others to tell Daniel that they need to destroy the podium clearly shows how little he likes the necessity.  Jack also shows understanding when he comes to get Daniel at the end – much like in Thor’s Hammer, he points out the harsh reality of the situation and then allows Daniel to absorb it and make his own choice.

 

The epilogue to this episode is very sweet.  I love the way Jack, Catherine and Ernest all gather round Daniel and try to cheer him up after they discover they can’t return to the planet.  Then we get the final two-shot of Jack and Daniel watching Catherine and Ernest hugging.  As with the shot of them helping Catherine through the event horizon, this shows how alike they really are despite the huge differences that have been pointed out earlier on.  They’re both total softies at heart, and it’s nice to see them standing shoulder to shoulder as the credits roll.

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Jack’s catchphrase finds a new use as a private aside, uttered under his breath.

On Ernest’s naked appearance:

Jack: Oh, for crying out loud…

 

Another characteristic saying crops up in the discussion of their situation on the planet:

Jack: Look, kids, we’re right on the edge of a cliff here.  I don’t think this place can stand another hit.

 

We also get catchphrase number two after Jack and Daniel come through the gate right at the end:

Daniel: Close one.

Jack: Ya think?

 

Best Bits

 

Characteristic lack of faith in the Pentagon at the beginning:

Daniel: The Pentagon said this was everything.

Jack: Oh, please!  The Pentagon’s lost entire countries.

 

Teal’c is still taking things rather literally at this stage.

After the DHD takes a nose-dive into the sea:

Jack: Okay, basic survival training.  We know what we have – what do we need?

Teal’c: We have the Stargate, we need the Dial-Home-Device.

Jack: Thanks, Teal’c.

This is echoed almost exactly in Prisoners, but with Daniel taking Teal’c’s role in the conversation.

 

Jack comes up with the solution to their problem in a classic Jack way – demonstrating his intelligence while still trying to make it sound like he doesn’t know what he’s talking about.

Jack: I’m obviously no scientist, but couldn’t we use that Ben Franklin thing?
