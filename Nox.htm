The Nox

 

Writer: Hart Hanson

Director: Charles Correll

 

Opens with the first real instance of pressure from the higher-ups for tangible gains from the Stargate programme.  At the risk of a lynch mobbing, I have to say I can actually see their point – running the SGC must cost a vast amount of money so we can possibly forgive the Secretary of Defence for wanting to see military results.  What’s interesting is the way Sam and Daniel respond to the suggestion that their missions so far have been pointless.  Sam extolls the scientific achievement of having visited 19 different worlds, while Daniel can’t understand how exploration could be seen as pointless.

 

Teal’c gets to play his “superior knowledge” card for once, demonstrating his worth to the programme by coming up with a destination that might provide technological gains.

 

So they set off and – dammit! – Daniel’s got his helmet back again!  With it comes the awkward shuffling and tendency to stand around with his mouth open.  It’s amazing how very different he can look just because of his headgear.

 

We get a lot of team discussion in this episode, which is nice.  Everyone gets to voice their opinion and Jack chairs the debates very effectively.  He plays Devil’s Advocate, making sure that Daniel can argue his case with logic as well as emotion.  For once, Jack listens to what everyone has to say without telling anyone to shut up.  He is calm, practical, sympathetic and ultimately decisive – great leadership skills.

 

Daniel gets to use a gun for the first time in the attack on Apophis.  He looks fairly uncomfortable with it, but I guess that’s understandable given his background.  The battle itself, however, is rather silly – the way they all leap out of cover in turn is totally ridiculous!  The first time I saw this episode, the fact that three of them get killed did come as somewhat of a shock.  Now, though, I just think that it’s nice Daniel has company the first time he dies (since the movie of course)!

 

When they wake up, we get a great Jack/Daniel friendship moment, with Daniel laying his hand on Jack’s hair and saying, “It’s okay, it’s okay.”  This is echoed by Jack later in Prisoners, and here makes a nice role reversal where Daniel gets a chance to give reassurance for a change.

 

The Nox are some of my favourite minor characters, Lya in particular.  I love the way she moves – her double-handed beckoning gesture is very sweet.  Nefrayu is absolutely adorable!  I’m not usually a kids-type person (actually I hate kids) but I’m totally with Sam here when she responds to his introduction with a delighted grin.

 

Daniel gets the opportunity to sample more white slop, though we don’t actually get to see him eat it this time.  It seems odd, though, since all the others get fruit – the Nox must have picked up on his liking for it!

 

O’Neill/Jackson combined eloquence comes up with the “Apophis is just very bad” argument.  It’s not surprising that Antaeus and Lya look singularly unimpressed by the guys’ attempt to persuade them of the danger.

 

The whole attitude of the Nox throughout is great.  They speak to SG-1 as if to rebellious children, which I suppose is how they view them.  However, their parenting skills leave a lot to be desired, since their refusal to explain their actions and instructions just make SG-1 even more determined to defy them.

 

Human arrogance has a lot to do with the problem, though, too.  The Nox made them disappear, brought them back from the dead and have never been detected by the Goa’uld in five hundred years – shouldn’t all this have given our intrepid heroes the tiniest clue that they are more advanced than they look?  But no, the Nox lead simple lives in the forest, so the humans automatically assume they are primitive.

 

More role reversal occurs when Jack starts making bows and arrows.  Bizarrely, Daniel suggests that continuing in their attempts to capture Apophis might not be a good idea.  He says to Jack, “You just don’t want to give up, do you?”  Considering Daniel’s suicidal determination to confront Apophis at every opportunity in Children of the Gods – and also earlier in this very episode – it seems strange for him to be counselling prudence now.  Usually, it’s Jack who has to try and restrain Daniel in these situations.

 

Jack demonstrates much better parenting skills than the Nox in his scene with Nefrayu.  Here we get the first of several instances of Jack interacting with a child.  He speaks to Nefrayu as to an equal and makes every effort to try to explain his actions and viewpoint.  This is a much better way of dealing with children, though of course it doesn’t actually work!

 

Antaeus vs Jack is a conflict that is never going to be resolved.  They are both convinced they are right, both extremely stubborn, and both completely unwilling to entertain a different point of view.  The only difference between them is that Antaeus responds to Jack’s arguments with simple, implacable statements, while Jack gets frustrated and starts shouting.

 

It’s weird rewatching the early episodes all in a row – once again, my preconceptions of the show are thwarted by Daniel calling Jack “Colonel”.  I was quite convinced before I started this whole reviewing project that this never happened, but clearly I was wrong!

 

They must have had real weather problems when filming this episode.  Throughout, you can hear the rain in the background, even though it’s not often visible.  Then, in the scene where they watch Lya being revived, all four members of SG-1 are clearly soaked!  It’s no wonder they all look fairly miserable – they’re wet through!

 

Use of names is important in this episode.  I really like the way Antaeus says, “Goodbye, Daniel” when Daniel is attempting to apologise to him.  This is the first time any of the Nox refer to one of the humans by name and it’s an effective way of demonstrating a greater connection to Daniel, as the peaceful explorer of the team.  Antaeus uses the same technique at the end when he says, “Before you go, O’Neill”, thus showing acceptance of Jack’s good intentions.

 

They really have to improvise on weapons for the second ambush of Apophis.  The log Teal’c is brandishing from behind a tree is so obviously plastic, it really makes me laugh!  Daniel gets a real chance to be Action!Jackson, though, since this time he ends up with a staff weapon.

 

Then we get the “lesson of the day” ending with Antaeus declaring, “Maybe one day you will learn that your way is not the only way.”  This always reminds me of those “moral of the story” bits they used to put at the end of He-Man cartoons.  They always irritated the hell out of me – as a child I was very resentful of TV shows trying to be educational when all I wanted was entertainment!  I can forgive the Nox their educational efforts, though, just because they are so cool!

 

 

Characteristic Moments

 

Nope. Nada. Zip.

Actually, Jack does say “zip” when he’s telling the others that the Fenri have no powers of their own – but I’m not sure that counts…

 

Best Bits – lots of these, though!

 

Face off between Apophis and Teal’c:

Teal’c: I die free!

Powerful stuff!

 

Upon revival:

Jack: Wasn’t I just –

Sam: Killed.

Jack: Killed as in –

Sam: Dead.

Jack: Dead.

Sam: Yeah, we know – we saw it happen.  Same thing happened to us.

Jack: Well… this is a surprise!

 

Jack’s reaction to the besotted smile Sam gives Nefrayu:

Jack: No, you can’t keep him.

 

When Antaeus is looking for Nefrayu:

Jack: I told him to go home.

Antaeus: I told you the same.  The very young do not always do as they are told.

 

When planning the final ambush:
Jack: So Shak’l thinks we’re unarmed, Apophis thinks he’s invulnerable.

Daniel: And they’re right – I think I found the flaw in your plan…
